1. Executive Summary

Liability Management Exercises (LMEs) emerged as a dominant restructuring strategy from 2020 onward, allowing distressed borrowers to restructure debt outside of bankruptcy by exploiting flexibilities (and ambiguities) in credit documentation. The key categories of LMEs include:

LME Type Landmark Transaction Core Mechanic
Uptier / Priming Serta Simmons (2020) Majority lenders amend credit agreement to permit new superpriority debt, subordinating non-participating lenders
Dropdown J.Crew (2017) Transfer of valuable IP/assets to unrestricted subsidiary beyond lender collateral reach
Double-Dip Envision Healthcare (2023), Chewy/PetSmart Intercompany loan from restricted sub to unrestricted sub creates two claims on same collateral
Non-Pro-Rata Exchange Various (2020-2024) Select group of lenders offered priority exchange, leaving remaining lenders structurally subordinated
Pari Passu Leakage Incora/Wesco (2022) Assets moved or new debt issued at subsidiary level, diluting existing lender claims

The documentation “arms race” between borrowers and lenders has produced increasingly sophisticated protective provisions. This guide catalogs every major countermeasure, its mechanics, known limitations, and current market adoption.

Critical Context for AMC: AMC Entertainment executed multiple LME-style transactions during 2020-2024, including exchange offers, new secured note issuances, and subsidiary-level financing (Muvico LLC, Odeon Finco PLC). Understanding these provisions is essential for evaluating AMC’s capital structure from a lender’s perspective.

2. Anti-Serta / Anti-Uptier Provisions
2.1The Attack: Uptier Priming Transactions

In the Serta Simmons Bedding transaction (June 2020), a majority group of term loan lenders (>50%) agreed to amend the credit agreement to permit a new superpriority tranche of debt. Non-participating minority lenders were effectively subordinated without their consent. The majority argued this was permissible under standard amendment provisions requiring only majority lender approval.

How it works mechanically: 1. Borrower identifies willing majority lenders (typically >50% but <100%) 2. Majority lenders amend the credit agreement to (a) create a new “superpriority” lien basket, and (b) modify the intercreditor/waterfall provisions 3. Majority lenders roll existing loans into the new superpriority tranche, often with a small new money component 4. Non-participating lenders are subordinated – their first-lien loans are now effectively second-lien

Key precedent: In Serta Simmons, the Southern District of New York initially dismissed the excluded lenders’ claims (2022), but this was later reversed on appeal. The case eventually resulted in a bankruptcy filing where the priming was contested. In Mitel Networks (2023), a similar uptier was executed. TripAdvisor/Liberty (2024) featured an open-market purchase twist.

2.2The Defense: Anti-Uptier Provisions

Modern credit agreements now include specific provisions to prevent Serta-style transactions. These provisions have evolved rapidly since 2020.

2.2.1 Sacred Right Expansion for Lien Priority

Provision: Any amendment, waiver, or modification that would subordinate the liens securing the obligations of any lender to any other debt, or that would change the priority of payment of obligations owed to any lender, shall require the consent of each lender directly and adversely affected thereby (i.e., “affected lender” consent, not just majority consent).

Sample Drafting Language (Post-2022 Market Standard):

No amendment, modification, or waiver shall, without the prior written consent
of each Lender directly and adversely affected thereby:

(a) subordinate the Liens securing the Obligations to the Liens securing any
    other Indebtedness, or subject the Liens securing the Obligations to any
    new or additional Liens (other than Permitted Liens) that are senior to or
    pari passu with the Liens securing the Obligations;

(b) subordinate the Obligations in right of payment to any other Indebtedness;

(c) alter the pro rata sharing or pro rata payment provisions of this Agreement
    in a manner adverse to such Lender (including by authorizing or permitting
    any "open market purchase" or "Dutch auction" or similar transaction that
    does not comply with the pro rata provisions herein);

(d) change the payment waterfall in a manner that would reduce the priority of
    any payment owed to any Lender relative to any other creditor;

(e) release all or substantially all of the Collateral or all or substantially
    all of the Guarantors (other than in connection with a transaction
    permitted by this Agreement);

(f) permit the incurrence of any Indebtedness that is secured on a senior
    basis to the Obligations without the consent of each Lender affected
    thereby.

Strongest formulation (2024-2025 “belt and suspenders” approach):

Notwithstanding anything herein to the contrary, no amendment, modification,
supplement, waiver, or consent shall:

(i) have the effect of (A) subordinating, or permitting the subordination of,
    the Obligations or the Liens securing the Obligations to any other
    Indebtedness or Liens, whether through an uptier, priming, or similar
    transaction, (B) allowing any creditor to receive priority in respect of
    any Collateral distributions over any Lender, or (C) otherwise modifying
    the relative priorities among the Lenders, in each case, without the
    written consent of each Lender adversely affected thereby;

(ii) for the avoidance of doubt, neither the Required Lenders nor any other
     group of Lenders (other than all affected Lenders) shall have the right
     to (A) subordinate the Liens on the Collateral securing any Lender's
     Obligations to Liens securing any other obligations, (B) subordinate
     any Lender's Obligations in right of payment to other obligations, or
     (C) contractually redirect any Lender's right to receive proceeds of
     Collateral or other amounts to any other creditor, in each case without
     the consent of each Lender directly and adversely affected.

2.2.2 “Affected Lender” vs. “Each Lender” Consent

Critical distinction: The strongest provisions require consent of “each Lender directly and adversely affected” rather than “each Lender” (unanimous) or “Required Lenders” (majority). This is deliberate:

  • “Each Lender” (unanimous) – Can be circumvented by buying out or “yanking” dissenting lenders
  • “Required Lenders” (majority) – Precisely the vulnerability Serta exploited
  • “Each directly and adversely affected Lender” – Targets exactly the lenders who would be harmed; cannot be circumvented without their specific consent

2.2.3 Known Workarounds and Limitations

Workaround How It Works Counter-Defense
“Open market purchase” exception Borrower argues that the exchange is an “open market purchase” not an amendment, thus bypassing sacred rights Explicitly include open market purchases and Dutch auctions in the sacred rights definition
Yank-a-bank to reduce blocking group Remove dissenting lenders via mandatory assignment provisions before seeking amendment Restrict yank-a-bank to situations where lenders have failed to consent to a request that requires only “Required Lender” approval; prohibit use to eliminate blocking positions for sacred right matters
Incremental facility carve-out Argue new debt is being incurred under an existing incremental basket, not via amendment Cap incremental facilities, require pari passu treatment, and include “MFN” protection
“Permitted Liens” basket Argue the new senior lien is a “permitted lien” Tightly define Permitted Liens; exclude any lien that would prime existing lenders
Non-guarantor subsidiary incurrence Incur debt at a subsidiary that is not a guarantor Require guarantor coverage ratio covenants; limit non-guarantor subsidiary debt

2.2.4 Market Adoption

  • 2020: <5% of new broadly syndicated loans contained explicit anti-uptier language
  • 2021-2022: ~25-30% of new BSL deals included some form of anti-Serta protection
  • 2023: ~50-60% of new BSL deals; becoming standard in direct lending
  • 2024-2025: ~75-85% of new broadly syndicated loans; virtually universal in direct lending (>95%)
  • Investment-grade credit facilities still rarely include these provisions (not viewed as necessary given IG borrower profiles)
3. J.Crew Blockers / Anti-Dropdown Provisions
3.1The Attack: Asset Dropdown to Unrestricted Subsidiaries

In the J.Crew transaction (2017), the company transferred its most valuable asset – the J.Crew trademark and intellectual property portfolio – to an unrestricted subsidiary that was outside the credit agreement’s restricted group. This IP was then used as collateral for new financing at the unrestricted subsidiary, effectively moving value beyond the reach of existing secured lenders.

How the dropdown works: 1. Borrower designates (or creates) an unrestricted subsidiary 2. Borrower transfers valuable assets (IP, real estate, key contracts) to the unrestricted subsidiary via a “permitted investment” 3. The unrestricted subsidiary is not bound by the credit agreement’s covenants or lien restrictions 4. The unrestricted subsidiary issues new debt secured by the transferred assets 5. Existing lenders lose collateral value; new creditors at the unrestricted sub have priority over the transferred assets

Subsequent examples: Travelport (2020), PetSmart/Chewy (2018-2020 – Chewy IP was at unrestricted sub level)

3.2The Defense: J.Crew Blockers

3.2.1 Restrictions on Designation of Unrestricted Subsidiaries

Core provision: Limit the borrower’s ability to designate subsidiaries as “unrestricted” when those subsidiaries hold material assets.

The Borrower shall not designate any Subsidiary as an Unrestricted Subsidiary
if, at the time of such designation or after giving effect thereto:

(a) such Subsidiary (or any Subsidiary thereof) owns or holds any Material
    Intellectual Property (including trademarks, trade names, patents,
    copyrights, domain names, or licenses material to the business of the
    Borrower and its Restricted Subsidiaries, taken as a whole);

(b) such Subsidiary owns or holds any assets (other than de minimis assets)
    that constitute Collateral or that were Collateral within the [12/24]
    months prior to such designation;

(c) the aggregate Fair Market Value of all assets transferred to, or held by,
    all Unrestricted Subsidiaries (together with all other Restricted Payments
    and Permitted Investments made in reliance on the Available Amount basket
    since the Closing Date) would exceed [specified cap / percentage of Total
    Assets];

(d) such Subsidiary generates revenues or EBITDA in excess of [X]% of
    consolidated revenues or EBITDA of the Borrower and its Restricted
    Subsidiaries.

3.2.2 Restrictions on Transfers to Unrestricted Subsidiaries

Core provision: Even if a subsidiary can be designated as unrestricted, restrict what can be transferred to it.

Notwithstanding anything herein to the contrary:

(a) No Loan Party shall, directly or indirectly, sell, transfer, convey,
    assign, license (exclusively), or otherwise dispose of any Material
    Intellectual Property to any Unrestricted Subsidiary.

(b) No Loan Party shall, directly or indirectly, sell, transfer, convey,
    assign, or otherwise dispose of any Collateral to any Unrestricted
    Subsidiary unless (i) such transfer constitutes a Permitted Investment,
    (ii) the aggregate Fair Market Value of all assets so transferred since the
    Closing Date does not exceed [X], and (iii) the Borrower delivers a
    certificate to the Administrative Agent certifying compliance with the
    foregoing.

(c) "Material Intellectual Property" means any intellectual property that is
    material to the business, operations, or financial condition of the
    Borrower and its Restricted Subsidiaries, taken as a whole, including
    [list specific key IP if identifiable].

3.2.3 Limitations on Permitted Investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries

The aggregate amount of Permitted Investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries
(calculated as the sum of (x) all Investments made in Unrestricted Subsidiaries
after the Closing Date and (y) the Fair Market Value of all assets transferred
to Unrestricted Subsidiaries after the Closing Date) shall not exceed the
greater of (a) $[X] and (b) [Y]% of Total Assets at the time of such
Investment, in each case measured at the time of such Investment.

Stronger version (2024+):

For the avoidance of doubt, no "Available Amount," "builder basket," "general
investment basket," or "ratio-based investment basket" may be used to make
Investments in, or transfer assets to, any Unrestricted Subsidiary if the
assets so transferred include (a) any intellectual property, (b) any equity
interests in any operating Subsidiary, or (c) any real property that
constitutes Collateral.

3.2.4 Known Workarounds and Limitations

Workaround Mechanism Counter-Defense
Licensing instead of transfer Non-exclusive license of IP to unrestricted sub (technically not a “transfer”) Include “exclusive licensing” and “licensing on other-than-arm’s-length terms” in the definition of restricted transfers
Gradual asset stripping Multiple small transfers each below the threshold Use aggregate lifetime caps, not per-transaction caps
Redesignation back and forth Designate as unrestricted, transfer assets, then redesignate back Require that upon redesignation back, all liens incurred while unrestricted must be discharged; impose cooldown periods

3.2.5 Market Adoption

  • 2017-2019: Only ~5-10% of deals post-J.Crew included specific IP transfer blockers
  • 2020-2022: ~35-45% included some form of J.Crew blocker
  • 2023-2024: ~65-75% of new BSL deals; standard in direct lending
  • 2025: ~80%+ of new leveraged loans contain meaningful J.Crew blockers; CLO managers now routinely require them
4. Anti-Double-Dip Provisions
4.1The Attack: Structural Double-Dipping

A “double-dip” occurs when a borrower creates structural layering through intercompany transactions that effectively give certain creditors two claims on the same pool of assets.

How it works (classic formulation): 1. A restricted subsidiary (“Sub A”) borrows from new creditors 2. Sub A makes an intercompany loan to its parent or an affiliate (“Sub B”) 3. The intercompany loan is secured by Sub B’s assets 4. New creditors now have (a) a direct claim against Sub A, and (b) an indirect claim against Sub B via the intercompany loan 5. In a bankruptcy, the new creditors effectively receive distributions from two entities on account of what is economically one claim

Landmark examples: - Envision Healthcare (2023): KKR-backed Envision used intercompany loan structures to create priority for new money investors, which was later challenged in bankruptcy - Incora/Wesco Aircraft (2022): Complex intercompany structures used in connection with priming transaction - Robertshaw (2023): Double-dip structure contested in bankruptcy

4.2The Defense: Anti-Double-Dip Covenants

4.2.1 Restrictions on Intercompany Loans That Create Priority

No Restricted Subsidiary shall make any Intercompany Loan to any other
Restricted Subsidiary or to the Borrower if:

(a) such Intercompany Loan is secured by Liens on the assets of the recipient
    that are pari passu with or senior to the Liens securing the Obligations;

(b) such Intercompany Loan would have the effect of providing the creditors of
    the lending Restricted Subsidiary with a claim against the assets of the
    recipient that is pari passu with or senior to the claims of the Lenders
    under this Agreement;

(c) the proceeds of such Intercompany Loan are used, directly or indirectly,
    to repay, prepay, redeem, or acquire any Indebtedness that is junior to
    the Obligations.

4.2.2 “No Double-Dip” Covenant

Comprehensive formulation (2024+ best practice):

SECTION [X]. No Double-Dip Financing.

(a) The Borrower shall not, and shall not permit any Restricted Subsidiary to,
    directly or indirectly, incur, create, or permit to exist any
    Indebtedness, Lien, or other obligation or arrangement that would have
    the effect of providing any creditor or group of creditors with:

    (i) both a secured claim against one Loan Party and a secured or unsecured
        claim against another Loan Party arising from, or in connection with,
        the same economic transaction or series of related transactions
        (whether structured as an intercompany loan, guarantee, contribution,
        or otherwise);

    (ii) a claim against any Loan Party that is effectively senior to the
         Obligations by virtue of being supported by intercompany obligations
         that have priority over, or are structurally senior to, the
         Obligations;

    (iii) an effective lien priority that exceeds the priority such creditor
          would have had if it had simply lent directly to the relevant Loan
          Party.

(b) For purposes of this Section, "Double-Dip Financing" includes, without
    limitation, any arrangement whereby:

    (i) a Restricted Subsidiary incurs Indebtedness and lends all or a
        portion of the proceeds thereof (directly or through one or more
        intermediaries) to the Borrower or another Restricted Subsidiary on
        a secured or guaranteed basis;

    (ii) the creditors of such Indebtedness would, in a bankruptcy or
         insolvency proceeding, have claims against more than one Loan Party
         arising from the same economic transaction; or

    (iii) the effect of such arrangement is to create structural or
          contractual priority for certain creditors over the Lenders with
          respect to the same Collateral.

(c) Nothing in this Section shall prohibit ordinary course intercompany
    transactions solely among Loan Parties that do not involve external
    Indebtedness.

4.2.3 Intercompany Loan Subordination

All Intercompany Loans from any Restricted Subsidiary to the Borrower or any
other Restricted Subsidiary shall be (a) unsecured, (b) subordinated in right
of payment to the Obligations, and (c) subject to a subordination agreement in
form and substance reasonably satisfactory to the Administrative Agent, which
shall provide that such Intercompany Loans are automatically subordinated to
the Obligations and that no payment shall be made on account of such
Intercompany Loans during the continuance of any Event of Default.

4.2.4 Known Workarounds and Limitations

Workaround Mechanism Counter-Defense
Contribution vs. loan Structure as equity contribution rather than intercompany loan Include “contributions” and “equity investments” in the scope of the anti-double-dip covenant
Multiple step transactions Break the double-dip into non-obviously-related steps Include “series of related transactions” language; anti-evasion provision
Non-guarantor subsidiary Use a non-guarantor subsidiary as the intermediary Require guarantor coverage tests; limit non-guarantor subsidiary debt
Upstream guarantee instead of loan Subsidiary guarantees parent debt, creating structural seniority without a formal intercompany loan Include guarantees in the definition of arrangements that create double-dip risk

4.2.5 Market Adoption

  • Pre-2022: Very few deals explicitly addressed double-dip risk (<5%)
  • 2023: ~20-30% of new leveraged loans included some form of anti-double-dip language
  • 2024: ~45-55% of new BSL deals; becoming standard in direct lending
  • 2025: ~60-70% of new deals; rapid adoption driven by Envision and Robertshaw outcomes
5. Cooperation Agreement Templates & Market Standards
5.1What Cooperation Agreements Are

Creditor cooperation agreements (also called “creditor coalition agreements,” “lender cooperation agreements,” or “creditor pacts”) are side agreements among groups of lenders that bind them to act collectively in response to potential LMEs. They are not part of the credit agreement itself but rather private contracts among lenders.

These emerged in force starting in 2020 when excluded lender groups in Serta-style transactions discovered that their lack of coordination was exploited by borrowers engaging in “divide and conquer” tactics.

5.2Core Components of Modern Cooperation Agreements (2024-2025 Standard)

5.2.1 Minimum Participation and Blocking Thresholds

SECTION 2. COOPERATION UNDERTAKINGS.

2.1 Minimum Participation. Each Participating Lender agrees that it shall not,
directly or indirectly:

(a) sell, assign, transfer, participate, or otherwise dispose of any of its
    Loans or Commitments ("Covered Holdings") if, after giving effect to such
    transfer, the aggregate principal amount of Covered Holdings held by all
    Participating Lenders would be less than [33.34]% of the total outstanding
    Loans (the "Minimum Threshold");

(b) consent to, approve, or vote in favor of any Proposed Transaction (as
    defined below) without the prior written approval of Participating Lenders
    holding not less than [66.67]% of the aggregate Covered Holdings (the
    "Required Participating Lender Threshold");

(c) enter into any agreement with the Borrower or its affiliates regarding
    any Proposed Transaction without the prior written approval of
    Participating Lenders holding not less than the Required Participating
    Lender Threshold.

Key calibration: The Minimum Threshold is typically set at a level that ensures the group maintains a blocking position (>33.33% for matters requiring supermajority consent, or >50% for matters requiring majority consent).

5.2.2 Definition of Proposed Transactions

2.2 "Proposed Transaction" means any:

(a) amendment, modification, supplement, or waiver of the Credit Agreement
    that would (i) alter the priority of the Liens securing the Obligations,
    (ii) subordinate the Obligations in right of payment, (iii) release all
    or substantially all Collateral or Guarantors, or (iv) modify the
    pro rata sharing or waterfall provisions;

(b) exchange offer, tender offer, or open market purchase of Loans;

(c) new money financing offered on a non-pro-rata basis to fewer than all
    Lenders;

(d) any transaction that would result in the priming, subordination, or
    structural subordination of any Participating Lender's Covered Holdings;

(e) any liability management exercise, restructuring support agreement, or
    similar transaction;

(f) any consent solicitation related to any of the foregoing.

5.2.3 Restrictions on Side Deals and Individual Settlements

2.3 No Side Deals. Each Participating Lender agrees that:

(a) it shall not enter into any bilateral agreement, understanding, or
    arrangement with the Borrower, any Sponsor, or any other creditor
    regarding any Proposed Transaction or any restructuring of the
    Obligations, in each case, without the prior written consent of
    the Required Participating Lender Threshold;

(b) it shall not accept any consideration (whether in the form of fees,
    consent payments, improved pricing, collateral, or otherwise) in
    connection with any Proposed Transaction unless such consideration is
    offered to all Participating Lenders on a pro rata basis;

(c) it shall promptly (and in any event within [2] Business Days) notify the
    Coordination Advisor of any communication, offer, or proposal received
    from or on behalf of the Borrower or any Sponsor relating to any
    Proposed Transaction.

2.4 "Most Favored Nation" Provision. If any Participating Lender receives or
is offered terms in connection with any Proposed Transaction that are more
favorable than those offered to other Participating Lenders, such more
favorable terms shall automatically be deemed to be offered to all
Participating Lenders.

5.2.4 Fiduciary Out Provisions

2.5 Fiduciary Out.

(a) Notwithstanding anything herein to the contrary, nothing in this Agreement
    shall require any Participating Lender to take or refrain from taking any
    action that such Participating Lender determines in good faith, after
    consultation with outside legal counsel, would violate applicable law or
    any fiduciary duty owed by such Participating Lender.

(b) If any Participating Lender determines to exercise its rights under
    Section 2.5(a) (a "Fiduciary Out"), such Participating Lender shall (i)
    provide written notice to the Coordination Advisor promptly (and in any
    event within [2] Business Days) describing in reasonable detail the basis
    for such determination, and (ii) cooperate in good faith with the other
    Participating Lenders to identify alternative courses of action that
    would address such Participating Lender's concerns.

(c) For the avoidance of doubt, economic considerations alone (including the
    desire to obtain improved pricing, fees, or other economic terms) shall
    not constitute a valid basis for invoking a Fiduciary Out.

Critical for CLO managers: CLO fund documents increasingly contain fiduciary obligations to maximize portfolio value. The fiduciary out must be carefully drafted to acknowledge these duties without creating a loophole. The 2024-2025 market standard explicitly excludes pure economic motivation from the fiduciary out trigger.

5.2.5 Information Sharing and Coordination Mechanics

2.6 Coordination Advisor. The Participating Lenders shall jointly retain [law
firm] as Coordination Advisor, whose reasonable fees and expenses shall be
shared pro rata among the Participating Lenders based on their Covered
Holdings.

2.7 Information Sharing. Each Participating Lender agrees to:
(a) share with the Coordination Advisor (subject to customary NDA
    protections) material non-public information received from the Borrower
    relating to any Proposed Transaction;
(b) participate in group calls organized by the Coordination Advisor upon
    reasonable notice;
(c) provide the Coordination Advisor with updates regarding any changes to
    its Covered Holdings.

2.8 Clean Team / MNPI Protocols. The Participating Lenders shall establish
appropriate information barriers and clean team protocols to address material
non-public information concerns, particularly for Participating Lenders that
also hold publicly traded securities of the Borrower.

5.2.6 Term and Termination

2.9 Term. This Agreement shall remain in effect until the earliest of:
(a) the date on which the aggregate Covered Holdings of all Participating
    Lenders fall below [25]% of total outstanding Loans;
(b) the date on which the Obligations are repaid in full;
(c) the date on which a bankruptcy or insolvency proceeding is commenced
    with respect to the Borrower (unless the Required Participating Lender
    Threshold elects to extend);
(d) [18/24] months after the date hereof, unless extended by Participating
    Lenders holding at least the Required Participating Lender Threshold;
(e) the written agreement of all Participating Lenders.

6. Sacred Rights Evolution
6.1Traditional Sacred Rights (Pre-2020)

Traditional credit agreements designated certain fundamental provisions as “sacred rights” that could not be amended without unanimous lender consent (or consent of each affected lender). These were narrowly drawn:

Traditional Sacred Rights (requiring each lender’s or each affected lender’s consent): 1. Reduction of principal amount of loans 2. Extension of maturity date 3. Reduction of interest rate or fees 4. Extension of payment dates 5. Release of all or substantially all collateral 6. Release of all or substantially all guarantees

Everything else – including lien priority, payment waterfall, pro-rata sharing, and subordination – could typically be modified by “Required Lenders” (usually defined as lenders holding >50% of outstanding obligations).

6.2Expanded Sacred Rights (Post-2020)

The LME wave demonstrated that the traditional sacred rights framework was dangerously inadequate. Borrower-side advisors could restructure the practical priority and recovery expectations of lenders without triggering any sacred right, so long as they did not technically reduce principal, extend maturity, or release collateral.

Expanded Sacred Rights in Modern Agreements:

SECTION [X]. AMENDMENTS AND WAIVERS.

(a) No amendment, modification, supplement, or waiver shall, without the
    written consent of each Lender directly and adversely affected thereby:

    Traditional Sacred Rights (Retained):
    (i)   reduce the principal amount of any Loan;
    (ii)  extend the final scheduled maturity date of any Loan;
    (iii) reduce the rate of interest or any fee payable hereunder;
    (iv)  extend the date for payment of interest or fees;
    (v)   release all or substantially all of the Collateral (except as
          permitted by the Loan Documents);
    (vi)  release all or substantially all of the Guarantors (except as
          permitted by the Loan Documents);

    New / Expanded Sacred Rights (Post-2020 Additions):
    (vii)  subordinate the Obligations in right of payment to any other
           Indebtedness (whether by contract, by amendment to the waterfall
           provisions, or by any other means);
    (viii) subordinate the Liens securing the Obligations to any other
           Liens, or permit the incurrence of Liens on Collateral that rank
           senior to the Liens securing the Obligations;
    (ix)   modify the pro rata sharing provisions of Section [X] in a
           manner adverse to any Lender;
    (x)    modify the payment waterfall provisions of Section [X] in a
           manner that would reduce the relative priority of any Lender
           vis-a-vis any other creditor;
    (xi)   amend, modify, or waive any provision of this Section [X]
           (i.e., the amendment provision itself -- the "anti-amendment
           amendment" protection);
    (xii)  permit any exchange, rollover, or conversion of Loans held by
           fewer than all Lenders into Indebtedness that is senior to, or
           secured on a priority basis to, the Obligations held by non-
           participating Lenders;
    (xiii) modify the definition of "Required Lenders" or any other voting
           threshold;
    (xiv)  modify any "yank-a-bank" or mandatory assignment provision in a
           manner that would allow such provision to be used to eliminate
           a blocking position with respect to any sacred right;
    (xv)   change the currency in which any payment obligation is
           denominated;
    (xvi)  amend, modify, or waive the provisions relating to the
           designation of Unrestricted Subsidiaries or transfers of assets
           to Unrestricted Subsidiaries [J.Crew blocker protection].
6.3Yank-a-Bank Interaction with Sacred Rights

The Problem: “Yank-a-bank” provisions allow the borrower to replace lenders who refuse to consent to amendments requested by the Required Lenders. Pre-2020, there was ambiguity about whether yank-a-bank could be used to eliminate blocking positions for sacred right matters.

The Fix (2023+ Standard):

SECTION [X]. REPLACEMENT OF LENDERS.

(a) The Borrower shall have the right to replace any Lender that (i) has
    refused to consent to an amendment, modification, supplement, or waiver
    that requires the consent of Required Lenders and has been approved by
    the Required Lenders (a "Non-Consenting Lender"), provided that:

    (1) such replacement shall not be permitted if the relevant amendment,
        modification, supplement, or waiver would constitute a modification
        to a Sacred Right provision (as set forth in Section [X](a)(i)
        through (xvi) above);

7. Pro-Rata Sharing and Waterfall Protections
7.1The Attack: Non-Pro-Rata Exchanges and Selective Offers

In non-pro-rata exchange transactions, a borrower selectively offers certain lenders (typically those willing to provide new money) the opportunity to exchange their existing loans into a new, higher-priority tranche, while excluding other lenders from the exchange.

Examples: - Serta Simmons (2020): Participating lenders exchanged into superpriority first-out loans - Boardriders (2020): Similar selective exchange with new money component - At Home Group (2021): Non-pro-rata debt buyback below par

7.2Anti-Non-Pro-Rata Exchange Provisions
SECTION [X]. PRO RATA TREATMENT.

(a) Each payment (whether of principal, interest, fees, or otherwise) and
    each reduction of Commitments shall be applied pro rata among the
    Lenders in accordance with their respective Pro Rata Shares.

(b) No Exchange Offer, Dutch Auction, Open Market Purchase, or similar
    transaction shall be consummated unless:

    (i) each Lender is given the opportunity to participate on the same
        terms and conditions (including price, exchange ratio, fees, and
        any other consideration) as every other Lender;

    (ii) if the transaction is oversubscribed, participation is allocated
         pro rata among participating Lenders based on their respective
         outstanding Loans;

    (iii) the Borrower has provided not less than [10] Business Days'
          prior written notice to all Lenders describing the material terms
          of such transaction.

(c) For the avoidance of doubt, no amendment, modification, or waiver of this
    Section [X] shall be effective without the consent of each Lender directly
    and adversely affected thereby.
7.3“Most Favored Nation” (MFN) Provisions for Exchange Offers
SECTION [X]. MOST FAVORED NATION.

(a) If the Borrower or any of its Restricted Subsidiaries consummates any
    Exchange Offer or incurs any Incremental Facility, and any Lender is
    offered or receives terms that are more favorable in any material respect
    than the terms available to other Lenders (including with respect to
    pricing, security, priority, maturity, covenants, or fees), then:

    (i) all Lenders shall be entitled to receive the benefit of such more
        favorable terms; or

    (ii) such transaction shall not be consummated unless all Lenders are
         offered the opportunity to participate on the same terms.

(b) The MFN protection in this Section shall apply to:
    (i)   interest rate and fee differentials;
    (ii)  priority and lien terms;
    (iii) maturity and amortization terms;
    (iv)  covenant packages (if the new debt has more favorable covenants);
    (v)   call protection and prepayment terms;
    (vi)  any other economic or structural term that is more favorable.
7.4Anti-Non-Pro-Rata Prepayment Provisions
SECTION [X]. MANDATORY AND VOLUNTARY PREPAYMENTS.

(a) All voluntary prepayments of the Loans shall be applied pro rata among
    all Lenders in accordance with their respective Pro Rata Shares.

(b) The Borrower shall not, directly or indirectly:
    (i)   prepay, repurchase, or redeem any Loans held by fewer than all
          Lenders, unless such opportunity is offered to all Lenders on a
          pro rata basis;
    (ii)  enter into any agreement with any individual Lender or group of
          Lenders providing for the prepayment, repurchase, or exchange of
          such Lender's Loans on terms not available to all Lenders;
    (iii) purchase Loans in the secondary market through any intermediary
          or affiliate, unless such purchases are conducted on the open
          market and all Lenders have an equal opportunity to sell.

Affiliate purchases Borrower’s affiliate buys loans from select lenders at a premium, then contributes to cancellation Restrict affiliate loan purchases; if permitted, require pro-rata offer to all lenders

8. Restricted Payments and Investment Basket Tightening
8.1The Attack: Basket Exploitation

Borrowers have exploited generous “Available Amount” baskets, “builder baskets,” and “permitted investment” baskets to transfer value away from creditors. In a distressed context, these baskets – originally designed for operational flexibility – become tools for value extraction.

Common exploit patterns: - Using the builder basket (which accumulates 50% of cumulative net income) to fund dividends to equity even when the company is operationally distressed - Using the “Available Amount” basket to make investments in unrestricted subsidiaries - Stacking multiple investment baskets to make aggregate transfers that exceed any single cap - Using “ratio-based” baskets (e.g., “permitted if Total Leverage < 4.0x”) with aggressive add-backs that artificially reduce the tested leverage ratio

8.2Available Amount Basket Limitations

Traditional “Available Amount” basket (borrower-friendly):

"Available Amount" means a cumulative amount equal to:
(a) 50% of Consolidated Net Income for each fiscal quarter ending after the
    Closing Date (or, if negative, minus 100% of such deficit);
    plus
(b) returns on Investments made with the Available Amount;
    plus
(c) net cash proceeds from equity issuances;
    plus
(d) the aggregate amount of Investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries that
    have been redesignated as Restricted Subsidiaries;
    minus
(e) amounts previously utilized.

Tightened “Available Amount” basket (2024+ lender-friendly):

"Available Amount" means a cumulative amount equal to:
(a) the greater of (x) $0 and (y) 50% of Consolidated Net Income for each
    fiscal quarter ending after the Closing Date (i.e., deficits do not reduce
    the floor -- but the basket does NOT grow during loss periods);
    PLUS
(b) [limited carve-outs as negotiated];
    MINUS
(c) amounts previously utilized;

PROVIDED, that:
(i) the Available Amount may not be used for any purpose at any time when
    (A) an Event of Default has occurred and is continuing, or (B) the
    Consolidated Total Leverage Ratio exceeds [X.X]:1.00;
(ii) the Available Amount may not be used for Investments in Unrestricted
     Subsidiaries in an aggregate amount exceeding $[X] (or [Y]% of Total
     Assets);
(iii) the Available Amount may not be used for Restricted Payments (dividends)
      in an aggregate amount exceeding $[X] in any fiscal year;
(iv) in no event shall the Available Amount be used for any Investment or
     Restricted Payment if, after giving pro forma effect thereto, the
     Consolidated Total Leverage Ratio would exceed [X.X]:1.00.
8.3Builder Basket Restrictions
The builder basket component of the Available Amount (Section (a) above)
shall be subject to the following additional limitations:

(a) Consolidated Net Income shall be calculated without giving effect to
    (i) any unrealized gains or losses on hedging or derivative
        transactions,
    (ii) any non-cash charges or credits resulting from purchase accounting
         adjustments,
    (iii) any extraordinary or non-recurring gains;

(b) the builder basket may only be utilized if:
    (i) no Default or Event of Default has occurred and is continuing or
        would result therefrom;
    (ii) the Borrower is in pro forma compliance with the Financial Covenants
         (if any) after giving effect to such utilization;
    (iii) the Borrower delivers a compliance certificate to the
          Administrative Agent certifying the foregoing.
8.4Permitted Investment Limitations

Anti-stacking provision:

For the avoidance of doubt, the Borrower shall not simultaneously rely on
more than one Permitted Investment basket for any single Investment. Each
Investment (or portion thereof) shall be allocated to a specific Permitted
Investment basket at the time such Investment is made, and such allocation
shall be irrevocable.

Restricted Investment categories (2024+ standard):

Notwithstanding the foregoing, no Investment basket (including the Available
Amount basket, the general investment basket, and the ratio-based basket)
may be used for:

(a) Investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries involving the transfer of
    Material Intellectual Property, Material Real Property, or equity
    interests in Material Subsidiaries;

(b) Investments in Unrestricted Subsidiaries in an aggregate amount exceeding
    the greater of $[X] and [Y]% of Total Assets;

(c) Investments in Affiliates of the Sponsor (other than Restricted
    Subsidiaries) unless on arm's-length terms certified by the Board;

(d) Investments the primary purpose of which is to move assets beyond the
    reach of the Lenders.

9. Market Trends 2024-2026
9.1Current Market Standard Provisions (What’s in New Deals)

As of 2024-2025, the leveraged finance documentation market has undergone a fundamental shift. What was once considered “aggressive lender protection” is now market standard in new issuances.

Tier 1: Nearly Universal (>80% of new BSL deals): - Anti-uptier / anti-Serta sacred rights - J.Crew blockers (IP transfer restrictions) - Expanded sacred rights covering lien priority and payment waterfall - Pro-rata treatment requirements for prepayments - Restrictions on yank-a-bank for sacred right matters

Tier 2: Common (50-80% of new BSL deals): - Anti-double-dip covenants - Anti-non-pro-rata exchange provisions - MFN protections for incremental facilities - Anti-stacking provisions for investment baskets - Enhanced reporting requirements for asset transfers to unrestricted subs

Tier 3: Growing but Not Yet Standard (25-50% of new BSL deals): - Comprehensive anti-dropdown provisions (beyond just IP) - Restrictions on intercompany loans creating structural priority - Enhanced cooperation mechanics embedded in credit agreements themselves - “Anti-Chewy” provisions (restricting subsidiary IPO proceeds leakage)

Tier 4: Emerging (<25% of new BSL deals): - Automatic springing cooperation mechanics upon distress triggers - Anti-aggregation provisions (restricting single lender from accumulating blocking positions for LME purposes) - “Anti-Incora” provisions (comprehensive structural subordination protections) - Mandatory mediation provisions before LME execution

9.3Rating Agency and CLO Manager Responses

Rating Agencies (S&P, Moody’s, Fitch): - S&P published updated criteria in 2023-2024 explicitly addressing LME risk in leveraged loan ratings - Rating agencies now evaluate documentation quality as a ratings factor – weak documentation provisions can result in a ratings notch-down or negative CreditWatch - “Recovery ratings” now incorporate LME vulnerability; weak docs = lower recovery assumptions - S&P introduced the concept of “selective default” treatment for certain LMEs, which can trigger CDS acceleration and CLO compliance issues

CLO Managers: - CLO indentures increasingly include “anti-LME” provisions: - Restrictions on CLO managers participating in uptier transactions without noteholder consent - Requirements that CLO managers not consent to sacred right waivers - Limitations on CLO managers entering into cooperation agreements that would restrict trading - LSTA (Loan Syndications and Trading Association) published model documentation in 2023-2024 addressing LME protections - CLO compliance testing now includes documentation quality assessments - Several CLO managers (Ares, Golub, Owl Rock) have publicly stated they will not invest in deals without minimum documentation protections

Key CLO-specific tension: CLOs face a conflict between (a) the economic incentive to participate in an uptier (getting priority position) and (b) fiduciary duties to all CLO noteholders and the broader portfolio effect of legitimizing LME precedents. This tension has led to litigation and intra-CLO disputes.

9.4Regulatory Developments
  • SEC scrutiny: The SEC has examined disclosure practices around LMEs, particularly whether selective exchange offers comply with securities laws when some creditors receive MNPI
  • Bankruptcy reform proposals: Several legislative proposals have been discussed (but not yet enacted) to address the treatment of LME transactions in bankruptcy, including potential preference and fraudulent transfer implications
  • Bank regulatory guidance: The Fed, OCC, and FDIC’s 2023 Leveraged Lending Guidance update references documentation risk as a factor in bank leveraged lending examinations
  • LSTA initiatives: The LSTA has been actively developing model credit agreement provisions addressing LME protections, with significant updates published in 2023 and 2024

Current frontier of the arms race (2025-2026): - Borrower-side innovation: Multi-step transactions that individually appear permissible but in aggregate achieve a priming effect; “cascading” LMEs where each step builds on the prior; use of “permitted debt” baskets to create competing collateral pools - Lender-side response: “Anti-evasion” provisions that look at substance over form; aggregate transaction testing; mandatory disclosure of planned multi-step transactions; “purpose” tests for basket utilization - Technology adoption: Lenders are increasingly using data analytics to identify documentation weaknesses before committing capital; law firms have developed proprietary LME vulnerability scoring tools - “Pre-nuptial” cooperation: Lenders forming cooperation agreements at the time of initial investment (not waiting for distress), particularly in direct lending
