1. J.Crew (2017-2020) – The Original Dropdown
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: J.Crew Group, Inc. – an iconic American apparel retailer operating the J.Crew and Madewell brands.

Sponsor: TPG Capital and Leonard Green & Partners acquired J.Crew in a $3 billion leveraged buyout in 2011.

What Went Wrong: - Post-LBO, J.Crew suffered from declining same-store sales, brand fatigue, and fashion missteps. - The retail sector faced secular headwinds from e-commerce disruption. - The company carried approximately $1.7 billion in funded debt against deteriorating EBITDA. - By 2016-2017, the company was overleveraged at 8-9x debt/EBITDA with limited liquidity.

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction (approximate, 2016):

Instrument Amount Priority
ABL Revolving Credit Facility ~$400M capacity First lien (inventory/receivables)
Term Loan B (First Lien) ~$1.3B First lien
PIK Toggle Notes (Unsecured) ~$500M Unsecured
Equity (TPG/Leonard Green) Deeply impaired Residual
Transaction Mechanics

The J.Crew transaction is the genesis of the modern dropdown LME. It occurred in two stages:

Step 1: Creation of the Structure (2016-2017) 1. J.Crew identified that its intellectual property (trademarks, brand names) was enormously valuable – the Madewell brand alone was estimated at $1B+. 2. The existing credit agreement had a basket permitting transfers of assets to “unrestricted subsidiaries” – entities outside the restricted group and therefore outside the collateral package securing the term loan. 3. J.Crew transferred its valuable IP assets (primarily the J.Crew and Madewell trademarks) to a newly created unrestricted subsidiary called “IPCo.” 4. This was done through a series of intermediate transfers, exploiting the “investment capacity” baskets that permitted the borrower to make investments in unrestricted subsidiaries up to certain thresholds.

Step 2: Raising New Capital Against the Dropped-Down IP (2017-2018) 5. IPCo – now holding the crown-jewel IP outside the reach of existing creditors – was used to raise new secured debt, with the IP serving as collateral. 6. The proceeds were used to retire some existing unsecured debt at a discount, effectively stripping collateral value from secured lenders and benefiting the equity sponsors.

Key Provisions Exploited: - Unrestricted subsidiary designation provisions - Investment baskets (General and “Available Amount” baskets permitted asset transfers to unrestricted subs) - Lack of “J.Crew blocker” language preventing the transfer of material IP

Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsors TPG Capital, Leonard Green & Partners
Company Advisors Lazard (financial), Weil Gotshal (legal)
Term Loan Lenders Broad syndication; Eaton Vance, Highland Capital, others
Term Loan Lender Counsel Various, including Akin Gump
Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Term loan lenders sued in SDNY (2017), alleging the IP transfer violated the credit agreement’s implied covenant of good faith and specific restrictive covenants.
  • J.Crew argued the transaction was explicitly permitted by the credit agreement’s baskets.
  • The case was ultimately settled as part of broader restructuring negotiations – no definitive ruling on the merits of the dropdown.
  • J.Crew filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in May 2020 (accelerated by COVID-19).
Final Outcome
  • J.Crew emerged from bankruptcy in September 2020 after a relatively quick restructuring.
  • Term loan lenders received equity in the reorganized company, with recoveries estimated at 40-60 cents on the dollar (significant impairment).
  • Unsecured noteholders received minimal recovery.
  • Equity sponsors (TPG/Leonard Green) were wiped out.
  • The Madewell brand was preserved as a central asset of the reorganized entity.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. The birth of the “J.Crew blocker.” After this transaction, nearly every sophisticated credit agreement was amended to include provisions restricting the transfer of material IP or “crown jewel” assets to unrestricted subsidiaries. Lenders must always insist on J.Crew blocker language.
  2. Unrestricted subsidiary definitions matter enormously. Lenders must scrutinize the conditions under which subsidiaries can be designated as unrestricted and the investment baskets that permit asset transfers.
  3. Sponsors will exploit every contractual gap. TPG and Leonard Green used a creative reading of the credit agreement to strip value.
  4. Collateral coverage is only as good as the covenants protecting it. Having a first lien is meaningless if the most valuable collateral can be legally transferred out.
2. Serta Simmons Bedding (2020-2023) – The Original Uptier/Priming Transaction
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Serta Simmons Bedding, LLC – the largest mattress manufacturer in North America, operating the Serta, Beautyrest, and Simmons brands.

Sponsor: Advent International acquired Serta Simmons in a ~$3 billion LBO in 2012.

What Went Wrong: - Massive disruption from DTC mattress competitors (Casper, Purple, etc.) - Traditional wholesale distribution model under pressure - Significant LBO debt against declining revenue and EBITDA - COVID-19 created an acute liquidity crisis in early 2020

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction (early 2020):

Instrument Amount Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$1.95B First lien
Second Lien Term Loan ~$450M Second lien
Total Funded Debt ~$2.4B ~10x levered
Transaction Mechanics

The Serta transaction is the landmark uptier/priming LME that reshaped the leveraged finance market.

Step-by-Step:

  1. Formation of the Majority Lender Group (mid-2020): Advent assembled a group of first lien and second lien lenders holding a bare majority (>50%) of the first lien term loan. Key members included Apollo Global Management, Angelo Gordon, and other large institutional holders.

  2. The “Open Market Purchase” Theory: The credit agreement required unanimous consent to subordinate existing debt. However, it contained a provision permitting “open market purchases” of term loans and incurrence of additional debt. The majority group argued these provisions could effectuate the transaction.

  3. The Uptier Mechanics:

  4. The company issued approximately $200M in new super-priority first-out term loans with superior priority to existing first lien debt.
  5. Participating majority lenders exchanged approximately $1B of their existing first lien term loans into new second-out term loans senior to non-participating lenders’ claims but junior to the new first-out money.

  6. Non-participating first lien lenders (~49%) found their debt effectively subordinated without consent.

  7. Post-Transaction Priority Waterfall:

Priority Instrument Holders
1st (Super-priority) New First-Out TL (~$200M) Majority group
2nd New Second-Out TL (~$1B exchanged) Majority group
3rd (Subordinated) Remaining Original 1L (~$900M) Excluded lenders
4th Second Lien TL (~$450M) Second lien holders

Key Provisions Exploited: - Section 9.05(g) – “Open Market Purchases”: Drafted to allow buybacks at a discount; the majority group argued it permitted exchange for differently-prioritized loans. - Amendment thresholds (majority, not unanimous) - Lack of explicit anti-priming protections in “sacred rights”

Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsor Advent International
Company Advisors Wachtell Lipton (legal)
Majority Lender Group Apollo Global Management, Angelo Gordon, others
Majority Group Advisors Gibson Dunn (legal), PJT Partners (financial)
Excluded Lenders LCM Asset Management, Ares Management, others (~49%)
Excluded Lender Advisors Akin Gump (legal), Houlihan Lokey (financial)
Litigation and Court Outcomes

Initial Litigation (SDNY, 2020-2022): - Excluded lenders filed suit alleging breach of credit agreement, implied covenant of good faith, and tortious interference. - Judge Jesse Furman declined to dismiss, allowing claims to proceed – an early win for excluded lenders.

Bankruptcy (January 2023) and the Critical Ruling: - Serta filed Chapter 11 in the Southern District of Texas (Houston). - Judge Christopher Lopez ruled that the uptier transaction was NOT a permissible “open market purchase.” The court found it was a private, negotiated exchange, not an “open market” transaction. This was the most significant judicial ruling on uptier mechanics to date. - Despite winning on the legal question, excluded lenders negotiated a settlement as part of the plan of reorganization.

Final Outcome
  • Serta emerged from Chapter 11 in June 2023.
  • Excluded first lien lenders received equity/cash recoveries estimated at 30-50 cents on the dollar – better than subordinated position would have given them, but still a major loss.
  • Participating lenders received somewhat better recoveries.
  • Second lien lenders received minimal recovery.
  • Advent International was largely wiped out.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. Sacred rights must explicitly protect against priming. Traditional protections (rate, maturity, principal) do NOT protect against subordination via new priority debt. Lenders must insist that priming debt requires unanimous or affected lender consent.
  2. “Open market purchase” provisions must be narrowly defined. Post-Serta, agreements increasingly define this very narrowly. Many now include “Serta blockers.”
  3. Majority rules can be weaponized. A bare majority can fundamentally alter the priority waterfall. Key provisions should require supermajority (66.7%) or unanimous consent.
  4. Being in the minority is extremely dangerous. Lenders who cannot join the majority face severe adverse selection risk.
  5. Even winning in court may not fully protect you. Excluded lenders won but still suffered significant losses.
  6. Sponsor incentives are misaligned. In distress, sponsor loyalty is to equity preservation, not creditor fairness.
3. Boardriders (2020-2023) – Oaktree-Backed Uptier
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Boardriders, Inc. – owner of Quiksilver, Billabong, Roxy, DC Shoes, and RVCA.

Sponsor: Oaktree Capital Management acquired the company through the 2016 Quiksilver acquisition out of bankruptcy and the 2018 Billabong acquisition.

What Went Wrong: - Significant acquisition debt - Competitive pressure in action sports retail - COVID-19 caused severe liquidity crisis as brick-and-mortar retail shut down - Levered approximately 6-7x debt/EBITDA heading into 2020

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$800-900M First lien
Equity (Oaktree) Impaired Residual
Transaction Mechanics

Boardriders executed an uptier modeled on the Serta playbook, with the critical twist that Oaktree was both sponsor AND lender:

  1. Oaktree, as both sponsor and significant lender, organized a majority group of first lien lenders.
  2. The company issued new super-priority debt with fresh capital.
  3. Participating lenders exchanged portions of existing first lien loans into senior-priority positions.
  4. Non-participating lenders were subordinated without consent.
  5. Oaktree itself participated as a lender, creating an acute conflict of interest: the sponsor controlled the company’s decisions AND benefited as a creditor at others’ expense.
Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsor and Lender Oaktree Capital Management
Excluded Lenders Minority term loan holders
Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders challenged the uptier, raising the additional issue of sponsor-as-lender conflicts.
  • The case was settled as part of broader restructuring negotiations – no final ruling on the merits.
  • Boardriders was ultimately sold to Authentic Brands Group (ABG) in 2023.
Final Outcome
  • ABG acquired the brand portfolio.
  • Non-participating lenders received impaired recoveries.
  • Oaktree preserved significant value through its dual position as lender and equity holder.
  • The company as a standalone operating entity ceased to exist.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. Sponsor-as-lender creates extreme conflicts. When the sponsor holds debt, they can engineer transactions benefiting both sides. Lenders should negotiate voting exclusion provisions for sponsor-affiliated lenders.
  2. The Serta playbook is replicable. Once established, other sponsors rapidly adopted the same mechanics.
  3. Brand-heavy companies are dropdown candidates too. Companies with valuable IP face both dropdown and uptier risks.
4. TPC Consolidated / TriMark (2020-2023) – Priming in Food Service
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: TriMark USA – one of the largest foodservice equipment and supply distributors, operating under TPC Consolidated.

Sponsor: Centerbridge Partners and other PE investors.

What Went Wrong: - Served restaurants and hospitality – devastated by COVID-19 - Revenue dropped precipitously as restaurants and hotels closed - Debt load became unsustainable as EBITDA collapsed

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Revolver ~$125M First lien
First Lien Term Loan ~$900M First lien
Second Lien Term Loan ~$220M Second lien
Transaction Mechanics

TriMark followed the Serta model during 2020-2021:

  1. Sponsor organized a majority lender group among first lien term loan holders.
  2. Company raised new super-priority financing from participating lenders.
  3. Participating lenders received priority treatment via exchange into a new senior tranche.
  4. Non-participating first lien lenders were effectively primed.
  5. The non-participating group was not offered participation on equal terms.

Key Provisions Exploited: Open market purchase/”Dutch auction” provisions, majority amendment thresholds, and lack of explicit priming blockers.

Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders challenged the transaction.
  • Largely resolved through negotiation rather than definitive rulings.
  • Added to the body of LME precedent, reinforcing the Serta pattern.
Final Outcome
  • TriMark underwent restructuring with non-participating lenders receiving diminished recoveries.
  • The company continued operations as foodservice recovered post-COVID.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. COVID created perfect LME conditions. Acute liquidity crises gave sponsors and majority groups leverage for aggressive transactions.
  2. Sector-specific risk amplifies LME vulnerability. Cyclical/event-sensitive industries face heightened risk that downturns trigger aggressive liability management.
  3. The majority/minority dynamic is the central LME tension. A bare majority can impose losses on the minority.
5. Incora / Wesco Aircraft (2022-2024) – Platinum Equity Contested Priming
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Incora (formerly Wesco Aircraft Holdings) – a leading aerospace hardware, chemicals, and electronic components distributor.

Sponsor: Platinum Equity acquired Wesco Aircraft in a $1.9 billion take-private in 2019, then merged it with Pattonair to form Incora in 2020.

What Went Wrong: - Wesco/Pattonair merger integration challenges - COVID-19 devastated the aerospace industry - Combined entity carried enormous debt from LBO and merger - EBITDA deteriorated significantly, leaving the company severely overleveraged - Platinum Equity’s management was criticized by creditors

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction (approximately 2022):

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$1.8B First lien
Unsecured / Second Lien Notes ~$1B Junior
Total Funded Debt ~$2.8B
Transaction Mechanics

The Incora LME (executed in 2022) was one of the most aggressive, combining BOTH the J.Crew dropdown playbook AND the Serta uptier playbook:

  1. Platinum Equity organized a transaction with select lenders and the sponsor itself.
  2. The company dropped down collateral – transferred assets to newly created subsidiaries outside the restricted group.
  3. New super-priority secured debt was raised at the subsidiary level, secured by the dropped-down assets.
  4. Participating creditors received positions in the new priority structure; excluded creditors saw their collateral coverage dramatically reduced.
  5. Platinum Equity invested new capital alongside participating lenders, arguing good faith.

Unique Features: - The combination of dropdown AND uptier mechanics made it one of the most structurally complex LMEs. - The sponsor’s direct participation gave Platinum additional leverage. - The transaction was executed very quickly, giving excluded lenders minimal time to organize.

Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsor Platinum Equity
Company Advisors Paul Weiss (legal), Houlihan Lokey (financial)
Participating Lenders Select term loan holders
Excluded Lender Advisors Gibson Dunn, Milbank, others (reported)
Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders filed multiple lawsuits alleging breach of credit agreement, fraudulent transfer, breach of fiduciary duty, and tortious interference.
  • Incora filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in June 2024 in the Southern District of Texas.
  • The validity of the priming transaction became the central contested issue.
  • Multiple creditor groups submitted competing plans of reorganization.
Final Outcome
  • Bankruptcy was prolonged and highly contested.
  • The company continued operating given the critical nature of aerospace parts distribution.
  • The case became a cautionary tale for sponsors using both dropdown and uptier tactics simultaneously.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. The hybrid LME (dropdown + uptier) is the most dangerous playbook. Credit agreements must block BOTH pathways.
  2. Speed kills the minority. Rapid execution gave excluded lenders minimal response time. Maintain real-time monitoring.
  3. Sponsor-driven LMEs are particularly aggressive. Equity preservation motives lead to more aggressive structures.
  4. Consider adding “Incora blockers” – provisions preventing the combined use of dropdown and uptier mechanics.
6. Mitel Networks / Citaux (2022-2024) – Non-Pro-Rata Exchange
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Mitel Networks Corporation – a Canadian-founded global UCC solutions provider.

Sponsor: Searchlight Capital Partners acquired Mitel in a $2 billion take-private in 2018.

What Went Wrong: - Market became highly competitive (Zoom, Teams, RingCentral) - On-premise solutions faced secular headwinds as market shifted to cloud - LBO debt was heavy; EBITDA underperformed - Levered approximately 7-8x debt/EBITDA

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$1.6B First lien
Second Lien Smaller tranche Second lien
Transaction Mechanics

The Mitel/Citaux LME involved a non-pro-rata exchange offer:

  1. Mitel (through affiliated entities including Citaux) launched an exchange offer for existing term loan debt.
  2. The exchange was not available to all lenders pro rata. A select group was invited to exchange for new, more senior instruments.
  3. Participating lenders received priority debt with better terms, enhanced security, and/or waterfall priority.
  4. Non-participating lenders were left with existing positions, now effectively subordinated.
  5. The structure characterized the exchange as “voluntary” rather than an “amendment” to circumvent consent thresholds.

Key Provisions Exploited: - The distinction between an “amendment” (requiring consent thresholds) and a “voluntary exchange” (which might not) - Open market purchase provisions - Absence of explicit prohibitions on non-pro-rata exchanges

Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders challenged the non-pro-rata nature, arguing it violated pro-rata sharing provisions and the implied covenant of good faith.
  • The pro-rata sharing clause became a central legal issue: did the credit agreement require that any repayment/purchase of loans be made ratably?
  • Court proceedings examined whether a “voluntary” exchange is functionally equivalent to a non-pro-rata repurchase violating sharing provisions.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. Pro-rata sharing provisions must cover exchanges. Traditional clauses may only cover “payments” and “prepayments,” not “exchanges.” Lenders must ensure explicit coverage.
  2. The “voluntary exchange” loophole is real. Sponsors will argue selective exchanges are not amendments requiring consent.
  3. Information asymmetry enables selective exchanges. Credit agreements should require disclosure obligations for any proposed exchange or repurchase.
  4. Sacred rights must include anti-subordination protections that cannot be circumvented through exchange mechanics.
7. Envision Healthcare (2023-2024) – KKR Healthcare Restructuring
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Envision Healthcare Corporation – one of the largest physician services companies in the U.S., staffing 25,000+ clinicians across 780+ facilities.

Sponsor: KKR acquired Envision in a $9.4 billion LBO in 2018 – one of the largest healthcare LBOs ever.

What Went Wrong: - The No Surprises Act (2022): Federal legislation eliminated surprise medical billing, a significant revenue source for out-of-network emergency physicians. - Post-COVID physician labor cost surge - Insurance companies used IDR process to push down reimbursement rates - EBITDA declined from ~$1B to well below $500M, leaving the company 15x+ levered

Capital Structure Pre-Restructuring:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$5.4B First lien secured
Second Lien Term Loan ~$1.3B Second lien secured
Unsecured Notes ~$600M+ Unsecured
Total Funded Debt ~$7.3B+
Transaction Mechanics

Pre-Bankruptcy LME Attempts (2022-2023): 1. KKR explored dropdown transactions and uptier exchanges. 2. The Ad Hoc First Lien Group organized early and aggressively. 3. The scale ($7.3B+ debt) and regulatory complexity made a purely contractual LME difficult.

Chapter 11 Filing (May 2023): 4. Envision filed for Chapter 11 in the Southern District of Texas. KKR’s equity was wiped out. 5. First lien lenders proposed debt-for-equity conversion, taking control of the reorganized company.

Creditor Group Dynamics: - First Lien Ad Hoc Group: Coordinated push for debt-to-equity conversion and majority ownership. - Second Lien Ad Hoc Group: Fought for recognition that their liens had value. - KKR: Largely sidelined; its $3.5B equity investment was wiped out. - Physician Practice Considerations: Corporate practice of medicine doctrines complicated restructuring.

Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsor KKR & Co.
Company Advisors Kirkland & Ellis (legal), Lazard (financial)
First Lien Advisors Milbank (legal), Evercore (financial)
Second Lien Advisors Paul Weiss (legal), PJT Partners (financial)
Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Valuation disputes between creditor classes were central.
  • Judge Christopher Lopez oversaw the proceedings.
  • No Surprises Act IDR impact on go-forward revenue was hotly debated.
  • Plan of reorganization confirmed with first lien lenders receiving bulk of reorganized equity.
Final Outcome
  • First lien lenders converted claims to equity, taking ownership (recoveries estimated at 50-70 cents via equity).
  • KKR lost its ~$3.5B equity investment – one of the largest PE write-offs in history.
  • Second lien and unsecured creditors received minimal recovery (low single digits to zero).
Lessons for Lenders
  1. Regulatory risk can destroy “stable” businesses. Healthcare companies are uniquely vulnerable to legislative changes. Lenders must stress-test for adverse legislative scenarios.
  2. Size does not protect against value destruction. $9.4B LBO backed by one of the most prestigious PE firms still resulted in near-total loss for most creditors.
  3. First lien lenders who organize early gain leverage.
  4. Mega-LBOs with aggressive leverage are fragile. 7-8x leverage at acquisition leaves no room for EBITDA deterioration.
8. Robertshaw (2023-2024) – Aggressive LME Playbook
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Robertshaw – manufacturer of flow control systems for HVAC, appliance, and water heating industries.

Sponsor: One Rock Capital Partners acquired Robertshaw in 2018.

What Went Wrong: - Post-acquisition operational/integration challenges - Supply chain disruptions and raw material inflation - Leverage became unsustainable as EBITDA missed LBO projections - Rising base rates increased refinancing risk

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$800M+ First lien
Other Secured Debt Various Secured
Total Funded Debt ~$1B+
Transaction Mechanics

Robertshaw is considered one of the most aggressive recent LMEs:

  1. Non-Pro-Rata Uptier Exchange: Available only to select lenders, providing new super-priority debt at a subsidiary level, enhanced collateral, and structural seniority through intercompany lending.

  2. Subsidiary Structure Manipulation: New intermediate holding company subsidiaries were created; assets and operations were reorganized to create structural priority for new debt. Value moved to entities where participating lenders had priority claims.

  3. Sponsor Co-Investment: One Rock invested additional capital alongside participating lenders as rhetorical justification.

  4. Exclusion of Non-Participating Lenders: Remaining lenders found themselves structurally disadvantaged with reduced collateral and effective subordination without consent.

Key Provisions Exploited: - Subsidiary designation and asset transfer flexibility - Absence of blocker language for non-pro-rata exchanges - Majority amendment provisions for collateral/guarantee modifications - The distinction between “contractual subordination” (might require unanimous consent) and “structural subordination” (might not)

Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders aggressively challenged the transaction, alleging the structural subordination was functionally equivalent to contractual subordination requiring unanimous consent.
  • Robertshaw ultimately filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy to resolve competing claims.
Final Outcome
  • The case demonstrated that LMEs increasingly lead to bankruptcy rather than preventing it, as litigation and creditor conflicts destabilize the company further.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. “Structural subordination” is the new frontier. When credit agreements block contractual subordination, sponsors achieve it through structural means. Agreements must restrict BOTH forms.
  2. LMEs can trigger a death spiral. The litigation, creditor conflicts, and reputational damage from a contested LME can push a company from “stressed” to bankrupt.
  3. Sponsor co-investment is not sufficient justification. The sponsor’s equity is often already worthless, so co-investment costs little but provides rhetorical cover.
  4. Credit agreements must restrict asset transfers to ALL affiliates, not just unrestricted subsidiaries.
9. Pluralsight (2024) – Vista Equity Double-Dip
Company Overview and Distress Context

Company: Pluralsight, Inc. – technology workforce development platform offering online courses and skills assessments.

Sponsor: Vista Equity Partners acquired Pluralsight in a $3.5 billion take-private in 2021 at peak tech valuations.

What Went Wrong: - Acquisition at peak multiple during low-rate environment - Post-acquisition tech sector downturn in 2022-2023 - Enterprise software spending cuts and customer churn - Revenue growth slowed dramatically - LBO debt unsustainable as EBITDA deteriorated and rates rose

Capital Structure Pre-Transaction:

Instrument Amount (approx.) Priority
First Lien Term Loan ~$1.7B First lien
Revolving Credit Facility ~$50-100M First lien
Total Funded Debt ~$1.8B+
Transaction Mechanics

Pluralsight is notable for its “double-dip” structure – a significant escalation in LME sophistication.

What is a “Double-Dip”? A structure allowing a creditor to recover twice against the same collateral pool – once through a direct secured claim and once through an intercompany claim or guarantee. The favored creditor “dips” into the recovery pool twice.

Step-by-Step:

  1. Creation of New Subsidiary Structure: A new subsidiary (NewCo) was created and certain assets/operations contributed to it.

  2. New Priority Financing at NewCo: NewCo raised new secured debt backed by contributed assets. Participating lenders provided this financing and/or exchanged existing debt into the NewCo structure.

  3. The Double-Dip Mechanic:

  4. New debt at NewCo was secured by contributed assets (first dip).
  5. NewCo made an intercompany loan back to the parent, and participating lenders had a claim against the parent through this intercompany loan (second dip).

  6. Result: Participating lenders could recover from BOTH NewCo assets AND claim against remaining parent-level collateral.

  7. Impact on Non-Participating Lenders:

  8. Competed for reduced collateral pool (assets contributed to NewCo).
  9. Additionally competed with the intercompany claim flowing back from NewCo.
  10. Recoveries diluted from two directions simultaneously.

Key Provisions Exploited: - Investment baskets allowing subsidiary transfers - Incurrence-based debt covenants allowing subsidiary secured debt - Absence of prohibitions on intercompany claims creating double-dip exposure - Majority amendment provisions for lien and guarantee modifications

Key Players
Role Entity
Sponsor Vista Equity Partners
Company Advisors Kirkland & Ellis (legal, reported)
Participating Lenders Select institutional investors coordinated with Vista
Excluded Lenders Remaining first lien term loan holders
Litigation and Court Outcomes
  • Excluded lenders challenged the double-dip structure aggressively.
  • Key arguments: impermissible subordination; intercompany loan mechanism was a fraudulent device; asset transfers violated restrictive covenants.
  • The case created novel legal questions not fully addressed by Serta precedent.
Lessons for Lenders
  1. The “double-dip” is the most sophisticated LME mechanic to date. It goes beyond simple priming to multiplying participating lenders’ claims against a shrinking collateral pool.
  2. Intercompany claims and loans must be restricted. Agreements must prevent intercompany loans that create senior claims at the parent level, subordinate intercompany claims to external lender claims, and restrict subsidiary lend-back arrangements benefiting select creditors.
  3. “Investment basket” stacking is a key risk. The ability to contribute assets to subsidiaries and lever them creates the double-dip foundation.
  4. Vista Equity’s playbook is replicable. This is a template, not a one-off.
  5. CLO managers and passive lenders are most at risk. Complexity means less sophisticated/passive lenders may not respond quickly enough.
10. 2025-2026 Frontier Deals and Emerging Trends
Credit Agreement Evolution – “LME-Proof” Documentation
Protection What It Does
Serta Blocker Requires affected lender consent for priming/super-priority debt
J.Crew Blocker Prevents transfer of material IP/assets to unrestricted subs
Incora Blocker Prevents combined dropdown + uptier mechanics
Double-Dip Blocker Restricts intercompany loans/guarantees creating multiple claims
Non-Pro-Rata Exchange Restrictions Requires any exchange/repurchase to be offered pro rata
Enhanced Sacred Rights Expands unanimous-consent items to include subordination, priming, lien priority changes
Voting Exclusion for Sponsor Debt Prevents sponsor-affiliated entities from voting on beneficial amendments
Key 2025 Trends

  1. Cooperation agreements among lenders. Lenders now enter agreements at facility inception, pre-committing to collective action and restricting individual participation in non-pro-rata exchanges.

  2. LME as bankruptcy trigger. Courts increasingly recognize that LMEs can be the proximate cause of bankruptcy. Several 2024-2025 cases involved companies filing within months of completing an LME.

  3. “Trap door” provisions. Majority lenders strip minority protections through sequential seemingly innocuous amendments that cumulatively eviscerate protections.

  4. The “Pari Plus” structure. Evolution of the double-dip: pari passu on existing claims PLUS priority on new money PLUS intercompany claim – a triple claim.

  5. Cross-border LME complexity. Using foreign jurisdictions (e.g., English law schemes of arrangement) to effectuate LME-like transactions that might be challenged under U.S. law.

  6. Software/SaaS LME wave. Following Pluralsight, multiple Vista Equity and Thoma Bravo-backed software companies from the 2021 LBO vintage face LME activity.

What to Watch in 2026
  • Appellate-level rulings on double-dip and structural subordination mechanics
  • LSTA model credit agreement provisions addressing LME risks
  • Potential targeted legislation
  • Continued PE sponsor creativity – each generation of protections is met with new innovative structures

Comprehensive Lender Playbook

A. Pre-Investment (Documentation Phase)

  1. Insist on comprehensive blocker language (J.Crew, Serta, Incora, Double-Dip, Non-Pro-Rata blockers)
  2. Tighten amendment thresholds – expand sacred rights to include subordination, priming, lien priority changes
  3. Restrict investment baskets – cap unrestricted subsidiary investments, prevent material asset transfers
  4. Strengthen pro-rata sharing to cover exchanges, purchases, and any non-cash consideration

B. During the Loan (Monitoring Phase)

  1. Track borrower subsidiary activity, asset transfers, designation changes
  2. Monitor sponsor purchases of debt in secondary market (LME indicator)
  3. Build relationships with other syndicate lenders
  4. Consider cooperation agreements
  5. Maintain current legal analysis of credit agreement vulnerabilities

C. When an LME is Announced (Response Phase)

  1. Organize immediately – form/join ad hoc group within days
  2. Engage experienced restructuring counsel and financial advisors
  3. Evaluate participation vs. opposition (cost-benefit analysis)
  4. If excluded, consider immediate injunctive relief before the transaction closes
  5. Choose litigation jurisdiction carefully (NY vs. TX vs. DE differ)

D. Macro-Level Considerations

  1. Limit single-borrower exposure; small holders are most vulnerable
  2. Recognize high-LME-risk sectors: retail, healthcare, telecom, media, technology (especially 2020-2021 vintage LBOs)
  3. Track sponsor behavior – sponsors who execute aggressive LMEs at one company will do so at others
  4. Price LME risk into spreads for credits with weaker documentation

Glossary of Key LME Terms
Term Definition
Uptier/Priming Creating new debt with higher priority than existing debt, subordinating existing lenders without consent
Dropdown Transferring assets from the restricted group to an unrestricted subsidiary outside existing creditors’ reach
Double-Dip Structure where a creditor has claims at multiple levels, recovering from the same collateral pool twice
Sacred Rights Provisions requiring unanimous/affected lender consent; traditionally rate, maturity, principal – now expanding
Open Market Purchase Provision allowing buyback of own debt; exploited in Serta to justify private exchange as “open market”
Unrestricted Subsidiary Subsidiary outside the restricted group, not subject to credit agreement covenants or lien requirements
Cooperation Agreement Agreement among creditors to act collectively against LME proposals
J.Crew Blocker Provision preventing material IP/asset transfer to unrestricted subs
Serta Blocker Provision requiring affected lender consent for priming debt
Structural Subordination Subordination via corporate structure rather than explicit contractual terms
Pro-Rata Sharing Requirement that payments/repurchases be shared equally among lenders in a class
Ad Hoc Group Informal creditor group organized to negotiate collectively
