1. Foundational LME Taxonomy
What Is a Liability Management Exercise?

An LME is any out-of-court transaction by a distressed or stressed borrower (often with participation of select creditors) designed to restructure the company’s debt obligations without a formal bankruptcy filing. LMEs exploit flexibility, ambiguity, or loopholes in existing credit documentation to achieve outcomes such as:

  • Maturity extension – pushing out near-term debt walls
  • Debt reduction – exchanging debt at a discount to par
  • Priority elevation – moving a creditor group ahead of others in the waterfall
  • Liquidity injection – raising new money secured by assets stripped from existing creditors
  • Covenant relief – loosening restrictions to give the company operational flexibility
The Three Generations of LMEs
Generation Era Defining Transaction Core Mechanic
Gen 1 2016-2019 J.Crew (2016) Asset dropdown to unrestricted subsidiary; IP transferred out of collateral package
Gen 2 2020-2022 Serta Simmons (2020), Boardriders (2020) “Uptier” / priming transactions; majority lenders vote to subordinate minority lenders
Gen 3 2023-2026 AMC (2024), Del Monte (2024), Mitel (2024) Hybrid dropdown + uptier; double/triple dip structures; cooperation agreement warfare; intercompany loan schemes
2. Creditor-on-Creditor Violence Tactics
2.1Definition and Mechanics

“Creditor-on-creditor violence” refers to transactions where one group of creditors exploits credit agreement provisions to elevate their own priority at the direct expense of other creditors holding the same or similar instruments. The “violence” is the value transfer from excluded creditors to participating creditors.

2.2The Uptier / Priming Transaction (Gen 2 Playbook)

Exact Mechanics:

  1. A borrower identifies that its credit agreement allows amendments with consent of “Required Lenders” (typically 50.1% of outstanding principal)
  2. The borrower secretly negotiates with a majority group of lenders
  3. The majority group agrees to provide new money and/or exchange into new super-priority debt
  4. Using their majority vote, they amend the credit agreement to permit the new priming debt
  5. Minority/excluded lenders are subordinated – their formerly pari passu claims now sit behind the new super-priority tranche

Credit Agreement Provisions Exploited:

  • “Required Lenders” amendment threshold – Most credit agreements allow non-sacred-right amendments with just majority lender consent
  • “Open market purchase” exceptions – Allow non-pro-rata debt repurchases, which the Serta court interpreted to include private exchanges with select lenders
  • Incremental facility provisions – Permit new debt under ratio-based or free-and-clear baskets
  • “Permitted Indebtedness” baskets – Allow new debt that can be structured as super-priority

Key Deal: Serta Simmons (2020)

  • Pre-transaction: $1.95B first lien term loan + $450M second lien term loan
  • Transaction: Serta worked with approximately 57% of first lien lenders and approximately 73% of second lien lenders to create new “super-priority” first-out and second-out tranches
  • Mechanism: Participating lenders exchanged existing debt at par for new super-priority positions and provided $200M in new money
  • Impact on excluded lenders: Non-participating first lien lenders saw their claims effectively subordinated behind approximately $2B of new super-priority debt
  • Fifth Circuit Ruling (December 2023): In LCM XXII v. Serta Simmons, the Fifth Circuit found that the “open market purchase” provision did not authorize the non-pro-rata exchange – it requires arm’s-length, market-based transactions. This was a pyrrhic victory because Serta had already filed Chapter 11 in January 2023, but the ruling has chilled future reliance on “open market purchase” provisions for uptier transactions.

Key Deal: Boardriders / Quiksilver (2020)

  • Oaktree Capital (majority lender) orchestrated a priming transaction
  • Provided new super-priority financing, subordinating minority lenders
  • Excluded lenders (including Brigade Capital) sued, alleging breach of the implied covenant of good faith
  • Settled confidentially, but set the template for sponsor-aligned creditor violence

Key Deal: TriMark (2021)

  • Private equity sponsor Centerbridge worked with majority lenders
  • New super-priority term loan was issued; minority lenders were primed
  • Court found the transaction did not violate the credit agreement’s express terms, even though it disadvantaged minority lenders
2.3The “Cooperative” vs. “Non-Cooperative” Creditor Group Dynamic

Cooperative Groups (Ad Hoc Groups)

  • Form early, often at the direction of a distressed debt hedge fund or the PE sponsor
  • Sign cooperation agreements binding members to act together
  • Negotiate as a bloc with the borrower
  • Advantage: First-mover advantage in shaping the LME; typically get best exchange terms, pro-rata participation rights, and new money opportunities

Non-Cooperative / Excluded Groups

  • Lenders who are either not invited, refuse the terms offered, or learn about the transaction too late
  • Left holding subordinated, stripped, or value-impaired claims
  • Defensive Options:
  • File breach of contract lawsuits (NY state court typical venue)
  • Use Delaware Section 225 proceedings to challenge board composition (novel Del Monte approach)
  • Appoint new agent and call defaults
  • Negotiate for settlement payments (often at par or par plus)
  • Accumulate enough debt to block “Required Lenders” threshold

Lessons from AMC and Del Monte (2024-2025):

AMC excluded 1L noteholders: Filed a traditional NY state court lawsuit alleging breach of the intercreditor agreement. Settled in July 2025 – excluded noteholders got to exchange into new third-priority Muvico debt, guaranteed by AMC Entertainment Holdings at par, plus the opportunity to provide new money.

Del Monte excluded lenders: Took the novel approach of replacing the agent with Black Diamond, calling a default, voting pledged stock to replace the board, and filing a Delaware Section 225 proceeding. This moved at extraordinary speed – two-day trial within approximately six months. Settled in April 2025 with excluded lenders getting par plus $20M in fees ($122M total repayment funded by new incremental facility from participating lenders).

Critical Lesson: The Del Monte excluded lenders arguably got a better outcome than participating lenders, who ended up tied to a company that filed for Chapter 11 in July 2025. Excluded lenders were paid at par and walked away clean. The term loan had been trading at about 75 cents on the dollar prior to the 2024 LME.

3. Cooperation Agreements
3.1What They Are

Cooperation agreements are contracts among creditors within an ad hoc group that bind them to act collectively in connection with a proposed LME or restructuring.

3.2Typical Provisions
Provision Description
Transfer Restrictions Members cannot sell debt positions (or only to buyers who agree to join the cooperation agreement)
Minimum Holding Requirements Members must maintain a minimum position size
Voting Commitments Members agree to vote in favor of the agreed transaction structure
Information Sharing Members agree to share MNPI within the group (creating trading restrictions)
Fee Arrangements Work fees, consent fees, backstop fees, and new money allocations are specified
Exclusivity Members agree not to negotiate separately with the borrower or other groups
Fiduciary Out / MAC Conditions under which members can exit the agreement
Milestones Deadlines for transaction completion; if missed, the agreement terminates
3.3Offensive Use
  • Blocking positions: Group accumulates enough debt (>50.1%) to control “Required Lenders” votes, then authorizes priming transactions
  • New money commitments: Group pre-commits fresh capital the borrower needs, extracting favorable terms
  • Information advantage: By forming early and hiring advisors, the group gets first access to company information and shapes the deal
3.4Defensive Use
  • Blocking minority: A group controlling >33.3% of any tranche can typically block sacred right amendments; >50.1% blocks any Required Lenders amendment
  • Mutual defense pact: Members fund litigation collectively, share legal costs, and agree not to settle individually
  • Anti-priming provisions: Group votes against any amendment that would subordinate its members
3.5Evolution (2022-2025)

Early Cooperation Agreements (2020-2022): Primarily defensive – minority lenders banding together to block unfavorable amendments. Simple structures focused on voting commitments and transfer restrictions.

Offensive Cooperation Agreements (2023-2025): Increasingly initiated by aggressive distressed debt funds seeking to control the restructuring. Include detailed term sheets for the target transaction structure. Feature “promote” structures where the lead creditor gets enhanced economics. May include provisions allowing the group to “drop” members who do not meet capital calls for new money.

Notable Examples:

  • Carvana (2023): Apollo and PIMCO formed a cooperation agreement to backstop an exchange offer that effectively subordinated other creditors, committing approximately $1.2B in new money in exchange for priority positions.
  • AMC (2024): Ad hoc group holding 57% of the term loan formed a cooperation agreement with the company. Per the Octus article, when Black Diamond (an excluded lender) reached out to Gibson Dunn (ad hoc group counsel), they were told non-ad-hoc-group lenders “could not participate in the transaction on a pro rata basis.”
  • Del Monte (2024): Ad hoc group holding 57% of the term loan cooperated with the company to structure a dropdown of substantially all assets into DMFC II. Members got first-out and second-out positions at par.
4. Sponsor-Backed vs. Creditor-Initiated LMEs
4.1Sponsor-Backed LMEs

How PE Sponsors Use LMEs:

Private equity sponsors have become sophisticated users of LMEs to protect their equity investments in distressed portfolio companies.

Sponsor Motivations:

  1. Preserve equity value: Even a small recovery on equity is worth fighting for given typical fund structures
  2. Extend runway: Push maturities out to give the company time to execute a turnaround
  3. Avoid bankruptcy: Chapter 11 typically wipes out equity; LMEs preserve sponsor control
  4. Extract value: Some sponsors use LMEs to extract dividends or management fees before an eventual restructuring

Sponsor Tactics:

Tactic Mechanics Example
Sponsor co-invest in new money Sponsor provides new capital alongside favored creditors, getting super-priority Envision Healthcare (KKR, 2023)
Subsidiary designation Sponsor causes borrower to redesignate subs as “unrestricted,” moving assets beyond creditor reach J.Crew (2016), PetSmart/Chewy (2018)
Management company transactions Sponsor’s management company enters into lucrative advisory agreements, extracting value Multiple PE-backed companies
Sponsor-directed cooperation agreements Sponsor coordinates with favored creditors to structure an LME preserving sponsor equity Envision, Team Health, Citrix

Key Example: Envision Healthcare (KKR) – 2023

  • KKR-owned healthcare staffing company facing approximately $7B in debt
  • KKR worked with an ad hoc group to structure a priming transaction
  • New super-priority debt issued to participating creditors; KKR contributed new equity to maintain ownership
  • Company ultimately filed Chapter 11 in May 2023, but KKR emerged with meaningful equity in the reorganized company – an unusual outcome for a PE sponsor in bankruptcy

Key Example: Citrix Systems (2024)

  • Taken private by Vista Equity Partners and Elliott Management in 2022 in an approximately $16.5B LBO
  • Heavy debt load (approximately $15B) and declining performance
  • Sponsors worked with creditors on an LME extending maturities and providing new capital
  • Complex amendments created new priority tranches; sponsors contributed additional equity
4.2Creditor-Initiated LMEs

The Rise of Creditor-Led Transactions:

Since 2023, there has been a marked shift toward LMEs initiated by creditor groups rather than (or even against the wishes of) sponsors.

How Creditor Groups Initiate LMEs:

  1. Accumulate a blocking or controlling position in one or more tranches
  2. Form a cooperation agreement with aligned investors
  3. Hire advisors (investment bank + law firm) to develop a restructuring proposal
  4. Approach the borrower with a proposal, backed by a threat (accelerate debt, call defaults, file involuntary bankruptcy)
  5. Negotiate with or around the sponsor – in extreme cases, seek to remove sponsor-appointed board members

Key Example: Incora / Wesco Aircraft (2022-2024)

  • Platinum Equity (sponsor) worked with a group of senior creditors to execute an uptier
  • A different creditor group (the “2024 Notes” group) was excluded and subordinated
  • Excluded group sued, alleging impermissible creditor-on-creditor violence
  • Company filed Chapter 11 in June 2024, where the court examined the pre-petition LME as a potential fraudulent transfer
5. 2023-2026 Structural Innovations
5.1The “Dropdown 2.0” – Asset Transfers to Unrestricted Subsidiaries

Classic Dropdown (J.Crew Model, 2016):

  • Transfer specific valuable IP/assets to an unrestricted subsidiary
  • Unrestricted sub issues new debt secured by transferred assets
  • Required exploiting specific “investment basket” provisions in the credit agreement
  • J.Crew used a $150M capped non-guarantor restricted subsidiary investment basket paired with a $100M general investment basket

The “Del Monte Black Hole” (August 2024):

This represents the most aggressive dropdown seen to date – substantially all of a company’s assets transferred with NO amendment to existing credit documents.

Exact Mechanics (from the Octus article):

Step 1: DMFI (the borrower) creates DM Intermediate, a restricted subsidiary excluded from the guarantee because it is not “wholly owned” – DMFI issued 100 shares of common stock to DMFI and 100 shares of preferred stock to Philippines-based Del Monte Pacific Ltd. (DMPL). Because of DMPL’s preferred stock, DM Intermediate was not a “wholly-owned domestic subsidiary” and therefore excluded from the mandatory guarantee requirement.

Step 2: DMFI transfers substantially all assets to DM Intermediate using Section 6.6(b)(ii)(x) of the credit agreement, which permits uncapped investments in “the Parent Guarantors, the Borrower or any Restricted Subsidiary.”

Step 3: DM Intermediate transfers substantially all assets to DM Intermediate II using Section 6.6(b)(ii)(y), which permits investments “by any Subsidiary that is not a Credit Party in another Subsidiary that is not a Credit Party.”

Step 4: Assets reach Del Monte Foods Corporation II (DMFC II), an unrestricted subsidiary, which issues new debt: - $269M Super-Priority First-Out (SPFO) - $467M Super-Priority Second-Out (SPSO)
- $139M Super-Priority Third-Out (SPTO) – for non-ad-hoc group lenders who contributed new money - $135M SPTO – for remaining non-ad-hoc group - Holdout lenders left with $106M at the original borrower level, secured by essentially empty entities

Credit Agreement Provisions Exploited: - Uncapped restricted subsidiary investment basket (vs. J.Crew’s capped $150M basket) - “Trapdoor” / “proceeds” basket allowing investments by non-Credit Party subsidiaries in other non-Credit Party subsidiaries on an uncapped basis - 60-day grace period for subsidiary guarantees – transactions completed before DM Intermediate was required to become a guarantor - Preferred stock to foreign affiliate – made DM Intermediate “not wholly owned” to exclude it from guarantee requirements - No amendment required – the entire transaction was accomplished within existing credit agreement terms, avoiding sacred right protections

Key Comparison to J.Crew:

Feature J.Crew (2016) Del Monte (2024)
Value Transferred $250M of material IP Substantially all assets of the credit group
Non-Guarantor Restricted Sub Investment Basket $150M (Section 7.02(c)(iv)) – capped Uncapped (Section 6.6(b)(ii)(x)) – investments in “the Parent Guarantors, the Borrower or any Restricted Subsidiary”
Proceeds Basket (Trapdoor) Can be paired with specified investment baskets only Can be paired with any investment basket
Jargon Produced “J.Crew Blocker” “Del Monte Black Hole”

Outcome: - Settlement in April 2025: excluded lenders paid at par plus $20M in fees - Del Monte filed Chapter 11 in July 2025 - Excluded lenders arguably did better than participants

The AMC Muvico Dropdown (July 2024):

Exact Mechanics:

  1. AMC Entertainment Holdings (AMCEH) creates Muvico LLC as an unrestricted subsidiary
  2. 175 domestic theaters (approximately 53% of TTM EBITDA) plus AMC’s IP are transferred to Muvico
  3. Participating 1L term lenders ($1,865M, 98% participation) exchange at par into new Muvico 1L term loan due 2029 (SOFR+600-700)
  4. Participating 2L PIK Toggle holders exchange $104M principal into Muvico TL and $414M into Muvico PIK Toggle Convertible Note due 2030
  5. New Muvico debt is secured on all AMC assets transferred to Muvico
  6. Muvico debt benefits from guarantee of AMC Entertainment Holdings (the parent)
  7. Excluded 1L noteholders (7.5% notes due 2029, $950M) are left at AMCEH level with claims on remaining assets

Key difference from Del Monte: AMC dropped down only approximately 40-50% of assets (vs. Del Monte’s approximately 100%), and AMC did NOT raise new money. The AMC transaction was purely a maturity extension mechanism, exploiting expansive value leakage and debt incurrence capacity under the excluded first lien notes’ documents. The transaction was pro rata as to the first lien term loans, so no “open market purchase” exploit was needed.

Intercreditor Agreement Dispute: The central legal battle was whether the ICA between 1L and 2L noteholders survived the transaction. AMC argued it “ceased to be effective” when 2L noteholders released their collateral. Excluded 1L noteholders argued the ICA remained because the notes were “refinanced” not “discharged.” The February 2025 excluded-noteholder brief argued the ICA “shall continue to be effective until the Discharge of Senior Obligations shall have occurred” and the notes were refinanced, not discharged.

Settlement (July 2025): Excluded 7.5% noteholders agreed to provide new money to AMC and exchange their notes for new third-priority debt at Muvico guaranteed by AMCEH at par. The bonds had been trading at approximately 90 cents on the dollar prior to the LME.

5.2“Pari-Plus” and “Super-Priority” Structures

Definition:

A “pari-plus” or “super-priority” structure creates a new tranche of debt that ranks ahead of existing first lien debt in the priority waterfall.

Structural Variants:

First-Out / Last-Out Structures: - All debt shares the same lien but with a contractual payment waterfall - “First-out” gets paid first from collateral proceeds - Often governed by an Agreement Among Lenders (AAL) rather than a traditional intercreditor agreement - This is exactly what Del Monte used – SPFO, SPSO, SPTO tranches all share the same collateral but with a contractual waterfall

Serta-Style Super Priority: - Creates an entirely new tranche of “super senior” debt - Ranks ahead of existing first lien by virtue of a new intercreditor agreement - Post-Fifth Circuit Serta decision, the “open market purchase” pathway is largely closed, so borrowers must find other mechanisms

2024-2025 Examples:

Mitel Networks (2024): Created super-priority first lien debt for participating creditors; minority lenders subordinated; litigation by excluded lenders.

DISH DBS / EchoStar (2024): Complex series of LMEs involving transfers of spectrum assets; new priority positions for creditors providing new financing; multiple lawsuits; involved FCC regulatory overlay.

5.3“Double-Dip” and “Triple-Dip” Lending Structures

Definition:

Double-dip structures allow a single lender to have multiple claims against the same borrower group, each secured by different entities or assets within the corporate structure, effectively multiplying their recovery in bankruptcy.

Exact Mechanics of a Double-Dip:

  1. Direct Loan (Claim 1): Lender makes a loan to the parent holding company, secured by the parent’s assets
  2. Intercompany Loan: The parent on-lends the proceeds to an operating subsidiary via an intercompany loan
  3. Structural Claim (Claim 2): The lender takes a security interest in the parent’s intercompany loan receivable. In bankruptcy, this intercompany receivable gives the lender an additional claim at the subsidiary level
  4. Result: The lender effectively has two claims – one direct against the parent, and one indirect (through the intercompany loan) against the subsidiary – for a single advance of capital

Triple-Dip Extension:

Add another level of intercompany lending through an intermediate holding company. The lender ends up with three claims in the group for one advance.

Credit Agreement Provisions Exploited:

  • Permitted indebtedness baskets allowing intercompany loans
  • Permitted liens provisions allowing security interests in intercompany receivables
  • Lack of specific prohibitions on multiple-claim structures in older credit agreements

Key Context – European Origins:

Double-dip structures were first popularized in European leveraged finance (particularly under English law credit agreements). They became more common in U.S. markets in 2023-2025 as lenders sought enhanced protections.

Deal Example: Altice France (2024)

  • One of the most prominent double-dip structures in recent memory
  • Complex corporate structure spanning France, Luxembourg, and other jurisdictions
  • Lenders structured intercompany loans giving them claims at multiple levels
  • When Altice France deteriorated, double-dip structures became central to creditor recovery analysis

Defensive Provisions to Watch For:

Modern credit agreements increasingly include: - “Double-dip” blockers prohibiting intercompany loans that create additional creditor claims - Limitations on security interests in intercompany receivables - “Anti-layering” provisions restricting creation of priority debt - “J.Crew blockers” limiting transfers to unrestricted subsidiaries

5.4Intercompany Loan Schemes

How They Are Weaponized in LMEs:

  1. Value extraction via intercompany lending: A borrower makes loans to affiliates or unrestricted subsidiaries, moving cash out of the credit group while creating receivables that are technically assets
  2. Priority manipulation: New lenders take security interests in intercompany loan receivables, getting a claim that is structurally senior to existing creditors at the subsidiary level
  3. Collateral dilution: The intercompany loan receivable replaces hard assets in the collateral package, as cash flows to unrestricted subs where existing creditors have no claims

The AMC LME structure involved intercompany relationships between AMCEH and Muvico. While the Octus article notes “intercompany loans not shown” in the transaction diagram, the structure created an intercompany dynamic where assets at Muvico were beyond the reach of excluded AMCEH creditors.

5.5The “Consensual Dropdown” – No-Amendment Model

This is perhaps the single most important innovation of 2024. Del Monte pioneered a dropdown that required NO amendment to existing credit documents. The entire transaction was accomplished within existing contractual permissions.

Why this matters: Sacred rights – which protect against amendments to payment terms and collateral – do NOT protect against actions taken within existing permissions. A company can strip substantially all collateral from existing lenders without amending a single document, if the credit agreement’s investment baskets are permissive enough.

This is why the Octus article labeled it the “Del Monte Black Hole” – the combination of an uncapped restricted subsidiary investment basket and a proceeds/trapdoor basket created a bottomless pathway for asset transfer.

6. Distressed Exchanges with Consent Solicitations
6.1Basic Structure

Typical “Carrots” and “Sticks”:

Carrot (for participating) Stick (for not participating)
Priority enhancement (new 1L position) Subordination (left behind with stripped collateral)
Higher coupon / PIK component Covenant stripping of existing instruments
Shorter maturity Remaining in a less liquid, orphaned tranche
New money participation rights No access to new money economics
Consent fees (25-100 bps) No fees
Par or near-par exchange rate Watching others get par while holding at market
6.2Consent Solicitation Mechanics
  1. Launch exchange offer: Borrower offers to exchange existing bonds/loans for new instruments
  2. Condition on minimum participation: Typically 50.1% or higher
  3. Paired consent solicitation: Participating creditors simultaneously consent to amendments stripping protective covenants from remaining (non-exchanged) instruments
  4. “Exit consent” amendments make old instruments far less attractive by removing collateral protections, restrictive covenants, events of default, and reporting requirements
  5. Result: Non-participating creditors are left with “stripped” instruments

Legal Guardrails:

  • Trust Indenture Act (TIA) Section 316(b): For bonds governed by the TIA, you cannot use majority consent to impair the right to receive payment of principal and interest – this is a sacred right
  • Credit agreements vs. indentures: Credit agreements typically have fewer statutory protections, giving borrowers more flexibility
6.32024-2025 Innovations

The “New Money Sweetener”:

  • Exchange paired with requirement to provide new money to participate
  • Only creditors willing and able to invest fresh capital get the best terms
  • Effectively screens out CLOs and other creditors who lack investment capacity
  • Creates a natural advantage for hedge funds and direct lenders with dry powder

Key Example: Carvana (2023)

  • $17B+ in debt from the 2022 ADESA acquisition
  • Apollo and PIMCO led a cooperation group
  • Exchanged approximately $5.5B of unsecured debt into approximately $4.4B of new secured notes
  • Non-participants left with unsecured claims
  • New money from Apollo/PIMCO paired with the exchange
  • Company’s subsequent operational recovery validated the aggressive exchange strategy
7. Lender-Led Restructurings
7.1The Shift to Creditor Control

Since 2023, there has been a decisive shift toward creditor groups dictating restructuring terms, often pushing sponsors to the margins.

Prerequisites:

  1. Concentrated debt holdings – one or a small group of funds holds a controlling or blocking position
  2. Willing co-investors who will support the lead fund’s plan
  3. Actionable credit agreement provisions – ability to call defaults, replace the agent, or take enforcement actions
  4. Capital to fund new money
  5. Management talent – ability to install or work with company management post-restructuring
7.2The Del Monte Endgame: Lender-Led Chapter 11 (July 2025)

This is a textbook case study in how an LME leads to lender control:

  1. August 2024: LME executed – ad hoc group gets first-out and second-out positions at DMFC II
  2. October 2024: Excluded lenders (led by Black Diamond) challenge the LME in Delaware Chancery Court
  3. April 2025: Litigation settled – excluded lenders paid at par plus fees ($102M for loans + $20M for expenses, funded by $122M new incremental facility from participating lenders)
  4. July 2025: Del Monte files Chapter 11 in New Jersey with a plan sponsored by the participating lenders
  5. Plan structure:
  6. $575M+ credit bid by participating lenders
  7. $412.5M term DIP facility ($165M new money + $247.5M rolled-up first-out claims)
  8. $125M exit financing
  9. Participating lenders to receive post-reorg equity
  10. DIP made available for all first-out term lenders to participate
  11. Result: The ad hoc group that led the 2024 LME positioned itself to own the restructured company

Key Lesson for Lenders:

Del Monte demonstrates how an LME can be the first step in a multi-stage lender takeover: (1) form ad hoc group, (2) execute LME to get priority position, (3) provide DIP financing in bankruptcy, (4) credit bid to acquire the company. Each step builds on the previous one.

8. Role of Distressed Debt Hedge Funds
8.1Key Players and Their Strategies

Tier 1: The Originators

Apollo Global Management: - Uses massive capital base to take large positions across the capital structure - “Loan-to-own” strategies converting debt to equity - Carvana (2023) – led the cooperation agreement with PIMCO restructuring approximately $5.5B - Uses insurance company capital (Athene) for uniquely long-duration, low-cost funding

Elliott Management: - Combines activist equity with distressed debt - Aggressive litigation; willing to fight protracted legal battles - Citrix Systems (co-owner, managed the 2024 LME from sponsor side)

Silver Point Capital: - Pioneered the “loan-to-own” model - Deep expertise in credit agreement interpretation - Active in numerous 2023-2025 LMEs, particularly retail and healthcare

Ares Management: - Combines direct lending origination with secondary market distressed investing - Sits on both sides – original lender and distressed buyer - Uses direct lending platform to originate rescue financing giving controlling positions

Tier 2: The Specialists

Black Diamond Capital: - Notable as the excluded lender representative in Del Monte - As agent for excluded lenders, replaced the board, filed Section 225, forced settlement at par plus fees - “Defense as offense” approach has become a model for excluded creditors

Brigade Capital: - Known for aggressive litigation strategies - Excluded creditor in Boardriders (2020); pursued litigation - Frequently invests expecting to use legal rights as leverage

How Funds Shape LME Tactics:

Information Advantage: Funds hire top legal counsel to analyze every credit agreement provision, identifying “loopholes” before borrowers or other creditors.

Capital Advantage: Funds with dry powder can commit new money – the most valuable bargaining chip. CLOs and passive investors typically cannot, creating a two-tier creditor market.

Speed and Secrecy: LMEs are typically negotiated in secret between borrower and ad hoc group. By the time non-participating creditors learn about the transaction, it may be too late to organize a response.

Emerging Trends for 2025-2026

Trend 1: “Pre-Baked” LME Provisions

New credit agreements now explicitly permit LME-style transactions – broader investment baskets, more flexible incremental provisions, weaker ICA protections. Implication: Diligence on credit agreement terms has never been more important.

Trend 2: “Anti-LME” Protections Becoming Standard

In response to creditor-on-creditor violence, new credit agreements increasingly include: - “Anti-Serta” provisions: Require all-lender consent for priority-changing amendments - “Anti-J.Crew” / “J.Crew Blockers”: Limit transfers to unrestricted subs, cap investment baskets - “Anti-Double-Dip” provisions: Prohibit intercompany structures creating multiple claims - Pro-rata sharing provisions: Strengthen equal treatment requirements - Cooperation agreement limitations: Some agreements now restrict offensive cooperation agreements

Trend 3: Litigation as a Feature, Not a Bug

Litigation is now expected in every major LME. Both sides factor outcomes into economic models. Settlement payments to excluded creditors are budgeted. Perverse incentive: Being excluded and litigating may produce better outcomes than participating (Del Monte).

Trend 4: NAV Lending and Margin Loans in Distressed Contexts

PE sponsors increasingly use fund-level NAV loans (secured by portfolio investments) to inject capital into distressed portfolio companies. This allows sponsors to provide new money without formally “re-equitizing.” Creates complexity for creditors because capital flow is indirect. Sponsors may use NAV loan proceeds to participate in LMEs alongside creditors, blurring the sponsor-creditor line.

10.1Framing for Management
Management Concern Response
“This is too aggressive” LMEs are now standard practice; the alternative (bankruptcy) is far more destructive
“We’ll get sued” Litigation is expected and manageable; settlement costs are factored into deal economics
“Our credit agreement doesn’t allow this” Detailed legal analysis shows specific provisions permitting the proposed transaction
“What about our PE sponsor?” Sponsor alignment is possible if they contribute new capital; if not, creditors have leverage
“This seems too complex” Break into clear sequential steps; each has precedent
“What if it doesn’t work?” The LME buys time; if the business fails, creditors are better positioned in bankruptcy
10.2Step-by-Step Proposal Framework

Step 1: Assess the Current Capital Structure

  • Map every debt instrument, its terms, and its creditor base
  • Identify “Required Lenders” threshold for each facility
  • Analyze all investment baskets, incremental provisions, and permitted debt capacities
  • Map intercreditor agreements and their termination provisions
  • Identify which creditors are “money-good” (can provide new capital) and which are constrained

Step 2: Identify the LME Opportunity

If the credit agreement has… Consider…
Broad investment baskets + uncapped trapdoor Dropdown transaction (Del Monte model)
Required Lenders amendment threshold (50.1%) Uptier / priming (with caution post-Serta)
Incremental facility capacity Super-priority new money tranche
Multiple debt tranches with separate ICA Cross-tranche exploitation (AMC model)
Weak restricted payments covenants Value extraction / distribution

Step 3: Form the Creditor Coalition

  • Target creditors holding >50.1% of the relevant facility
  • Hire advisors: investment bank (PJT, Houlihan Lokey, Evercore, Moelis) and law firm (Weil, Paul Weiss, Milbank, Gibson Dunn)
  • Execute cooperation agreement with transfer restrictions, voting commitments, and fee arrangements

Step 4: Negotiate with the Company

  • Present the LME structure to management and board
  • Emphasize benefits: maturity extension, liquidity injection, covenant relief
  • Offer a “carrot”: reduced interest burden, additional liquidity
  • Make clear the “stick”: defaults, blocked refinancings, involuntary bankruptcy

Step 5: Execute the Transaction

  • Draft definitive documentation
  • Launch exchange offer and/or consent solicitation
  • Obtain Required Lenders consent for necessary amendments
  • Complete asset transfers, debt exchanges, and new money advances
  • Prepare for litigation from excluded creditors

Step 6: Manage the Fallout

  • Budget for litigation costs and potential settlement payments
  • Monitor post-LME performance
  • Be prepared for Phase 2 (Chapter 11) if the LME proves to be a bridge to bankruptcy

Appendix A: Glossary of Key LME Terms
Term Definition
Ad Hoc Group Informal group of creditors who band together (usually with counsel) to negotiate collectively
AOSA All or Substantially All – sacred right provision protecting against release of all/substantially all collateral
Blocking Position Holding enough debt to veto Required Lenders amendments (typically >49.9%)
Consent Fee Payment to creditors who agree to an amendment or exchange
Cooperation Agreement Contract among creditors committing to act collectively
Credit Party A borrower or guarantor under a credit agreement
DIP Financing Debtor-in-possession financing provided during Chapter 11
Dropdown Transfer of assets from a restricted to an unrestricted subsidiary
Exit Consent Amendment to existing instruments obtained as condition of participating in an exchange
First-Out / Last-Out Payment waterfall priority within the same lien
Incremental Facility Additional debt capacity permitted under a credit agreement
Intercreditor Agreement (ICA) Contract governing relative rights of different creditor classes
Investment Basket Covenant provision permitting specified investments (including intercompany transfers)
Loan-to-Own Strategy of acquiring debt to convert to equity through restructuring
Non-Pro-Rata Treatment favoring certain creditors over others within the same class
Open Market Purchase Credit agreement provision permitting debt repurchases in open market transactions
Pari Passu Equal priority / ranking
PIK (Payment-in-Kind) Interest paid by issuing additional debt rather than cash
Priming Creating new debt ranking ahead of existing debt
Required Lenders Threshold (usually 50.1%) needed to approve amendments
Sacred Rights Provisions requiring unanimous or affected-lender consent
Super-Priority Debt ranking ahead of all existing secured debt
Trapdoor Basket Investment basket permitting indirect transfers through chains of subsidiaries
Unrestricted Subsidiary Subsidiary not subject to the credit agreement’s covenants
Uptier Transaction elevating certain creditors above others in priority
