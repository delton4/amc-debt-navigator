Comprehensive Research for AMC Entertainment Case Competition

Context: You are a creditor group pitching a creative and feasible restructuring solution to AMC Entertainment management (the judges). Your proposal must simultaneously protect lender recovery AND be attractive enough for management/board to agree.

1. Lender-Led Restructuring Mechanics
How Creditor Groups Initiate and Drive Out-of-Court Restructurings

The process follows a well-established sequence that typically unfolds over 4-12 weeks from initial formation to term sheet:

Phase 1: Identifying Distress and Forming the Ad Hoc Group (Weeks 1-3)

  • Trigger events: Debt trading below par (AMC’s debt was trading at 75-90 cents pre-LME), upcoming maturity wall, covenant breaches, deteriorating EBITDA, liquidity runway shortening below 12-18 months
  • Initial creditor coordination: One or more sophisticated creditors (typically distressed hedge funds or CLO managers) begin informal outreach to other holders of the same tranche
  • Engaging counsel: The nascent group retains restructuring counsel who establishes a “common interest” privilege to allow information sharing
  • Forming the ad hoc group: Once holders representing a critical mass (typically 33-50%+) of a given tranche are assembled, they formalize via a cooperation agreement
  • Public vs. private: The group files a Rule 13d notice (for bonds) or may remain private (for loans). Going public signals seriousness but limits trading flexibility
  • NDA and information access: The group requests the company enter into an NDA and provide non-public financial information, which begins the formal engagement

Phase 2: Analysis and Term Sheet Development (Weeks 3-6)

  • Group’s financial advisor builds an independent model of the company’s business, liquidity, and recovery waterfall
  • Legal counsel analyzes credit documents for: permitted investment baskets, incurrence capacity, restricted payment covenants, sacred rights, intercreditor agreement provisions, and “loose” language that creates LME opportunities
  • The group develops a “term sheet” outlining the proposed restructuring transaction
  • Internal group economics are negotiated (who contributes new money, how early consent fees are allocated, etc.)

Phase 3: Engagement with the Company (Weeks 5-10)

  • The ad hoc group approaches the company (typically through company-side advisors) with the term sheet
  • Negotiation ensues – this is an iterative process where terms are adjusted
  • The company may run a parallel process soliciting competing proposals from other creditor groups
  • Key leverage point: the group’s ability to block alternatives (if they hold enough debt) vs. the company’s ability to play creditor groups against each other

Phase 4: Documentation and Execution (Weeks 8-12+)

  • Once principal terms are agreed, legal counsel drafts the definitive documents
  • For exchange offers: preparation of offering memorandum, registration statements (if applicable), and amended credit documents
  • For amendments: preparation of consent solicitation materials
  • Execution: typically a 20-30 business day solicitation/exchange period
  • Closing conditions, minimum participation thresholds, and backstop arrangements are finalized

How Lenders Select and Engage with Company Management

The approach matters enormously. The most successful creditor-driven restructurings involve early, constructive engagement rather than adversarial confrontation:

  1. Initial contact: Typically through the company’s existing financial or legal advisors, or directly to the CFO/General Counsel. A letter from the ad hoc group’s counsel announcing the group’s formation and requesting engagement is the standard opening move.
  2. Information request: The group requests access to non-public information under an NDA. This is a critical test – companies that refuse to engage early often face more aggressive creditor tactics later.
  3. Collaborative framing: The initial communication should frame the group as a “solution provider” – “We are prepared to provide new capital and extend the company’s runway” is far more effective than “We intend to enforce our contractual rights.”
  4. Management meeting: Once an NDA is in place, a face-to-face meeting between the ad hoc group (typically 2-3 principals plus advisors) and management (CEO, CFO, General Counsel, plus company advisors) focuses on understanding management’s business plan and where the capital structure needs to go.
2. Aligning Lender and Management Incentives
The Fundamental Tension

Lenders want to maximize recovery on their claims. Management wants to preserve the enterprise, maintain their positions, and protect equity value. These interests are not inherently opposed – in fact, they often align more than either side initially realizes.

The key insight for your case competition: The most successful LME proposals present management with a “better alternative to the current path.” If you can show that your proposal (a) extends the company’s runway, (b) reduces near-term cash obligations, (c) preserves management’s roles, and (d) avoids the stigma and disruption of bankruptcy, management has strong incentives to agree.

Why Management Might Agree to an LME
  1. Avoiding Chapter 11: Management faces loss of control (potential CRO or trustee), intense scrutiny, professional stigma, potential personal liability, near-certain equity wipe-out, and rejection of employment contracts.
  2. Maturity wall relief: AMC faced nearly $3 billion in debt maturing before end of 2026. An LME that extends maturities gives management time to execute its business plan.
  3. Reduced cash interest burden: Converting cash-pay to PIK or reducing overall interest expense frees cash flow for operations.
  4. Liquidity injection: If the LME includes new money (as Del Monte’s did with $240M), management gets desperately needed operating capital.
  5. Maintaining going concern value: AMC’s theaters have long-term leases, studio relationships, and brand value worth far more as a going concern than in liquidation.
  6. Preserving management equity: In many LMEs, management’s equity is preserved or enhanced through MIPs, whereas in Chapter 11, existing equity is typically wiped out.
“Carrot” Approaches – What Lenders Can Offer Management

1. New Money Provision - Commit to inject fresh capital (new term loans, revolving credit, or new notes) for operations - Often the single most compelling element because it solves the immediate liquidity crisis - Structure as a first-out tranche (highest repayment priority) giving lenders downside protection while providing the company cash - Example: Del Monte’s LME raised $240M of new money through drop-down financing

2. Management Incentive Plans (MIPs) - Offer management equity in the restructured company - Typical MIP pool: 5-10% of post-restructuring equity - Include restricted stock, options, phantom equity, or profit-sharing - Vesting: 3-4 years with time-based and performance-based triggers plus some upfront vesting for good faith - This is critically important: Management’s willingness to cooperate correlates directly with whether they have an economic stake in the outcome

3. Covenant Relief - Relax financial covenants (leverage ratios, interest coverage, minimum liquidity) that the company is in danger of tripping - Gives management operational breathing room - In exchange, lenders get enhanced information rights and consent rights

4. Maturity Extension - Extending maturities from, say, 2026 to 2029-2031 removes the near-term refinancing cliff - This was the primary objective of AMC’s July 2024 LME – reducing 2-year maturities from $2,781M to $362M

5. Reduced Cash Interest Burden - Converting some cash interest to PIK reduces cash outflows - AMC’s Muvico 6.00%/8.00% Cash/PIK Toggle Notes allow choosing PIK when cash is tight - Lenders sacrifice current yield but get higher total return (PIK compounds on larger principal)

6. Operational Support - Creditor-designated board members can be framed as “adding experienced directors” rather than “creditor oversight” - Large distressed funds can offer operational expertise, industry contacts, and strategic guidance

“Stick” Approaches – Leverage Points for Lenders

1. Acceleration / Default Threats - If the company is in default, lenders holding the Required Lender threshold (typically 50%+) can accelerate, triggering cross-defaults across the capital structure - The threat is more powerful than the action. Actually accelerating often destroys value. The credible threat drives management to negotiate.

2. Covenant Enforcement - Refuse waivers for covenant violations, limiting operational flexibility - Block consent for asset sales, acquisitions, additional debt, or restricted payments

3. Blocking DIP Financing - A creditor group controlling enough debt can control who provides DIP financing in any bankruptcy - By offering or threatening to withhold DIP financing, lenders shape in-court restructuring terms - Example: Del Monte’s participating lenders provided the DIP facility when it filed Ch.11 in July 2025

4. Controlling the Narrative - Organized creditors with sophisticated advisors can shape market perception - A well-publicized creditor proposal pressures management to respond constructively

5. Litigation Threats - Breach of credit agreement claims (AMC excluded 1L noteholders filed in NY state court, Sept 2024) - Breach of intercreditor agreement claims - Delaware Section 225 actions to replace board members (Del Monte excluded lenders did this through Black Diamond) - Fraudulent transfer and fiduciary duty claims

6. Involuntary Bankruptcy - Three or more creditors with qualifying claims can file an involuntary petition - Nuclear option rarely used, but the threat is powerful

How Sponsors Factor In (AMC-Specific)

AMC has no PE sponsor. It is publicly traded with diffuse retail and institutional shareholders. Management is accountable to the board, which has fiduciary duties to shareholders (but in the zone of insolvency, duties shift to include creditors). CEO Adam Aron has strong personal incentives tied to compensation, reputation, and the “meme stock” retail base.

Strategic implication for your pitch: With no PE sponsor to negotiate with, your proposal must appeal directly to the board’s fiduciary duties and management’s self-interest. The board is the key decision-maker.

3. Transaction Structures That Serve Both Sides
Structure 1: Exchange Offers
  • Existing creditors exchange current debt for new instruments with different terms (lower principal, longer maturity, different rate, different priority, equity conversion, or a combination)
  • Lender benefit: Exchange into higher-priority debt; tighter covenants and better security; non-participating lenders left subordinated
  • Company benefit: Reduced face value (deleveraging), extended maturities, reduced cash interest, avoids bankruptcy
  • AMC Precedent: The July 2024 LME: participating 1L term lenders exchanged at par into new 1L Muvico debt; participating 2L noteholders exchanged into 1L/2L Muvico mix at par; excluded 1L noteholders left behind
  • Key design: Minimum participation threshold (50-66%), early bird consent fee (50-100 bps), and pro-rata vs. non-pro-rata structure (non-pro-rata is more aggressive, invites litigation)
Structure 2: New Money + Exchange Combination
  • Creditors provide fresh capital AND exchange existing debt into new instruments
  • New money at super-priority (first-out); existing debt exchanged into second-out or lower
  • Lender benefit: Attractive yield (SOFR + 500-800 bps) with first-priority repayment; OID on new money provides immediate gain
  • Company benefit: Fresh liquidity (the most compelling element), maturity extension, demonstrates investor confidence
  • Typical AMC structure:
  • New Money: First-Out 1L+ at attractive spread
  • Exchanged 1L: Second-Out
  • Exchanged 2L: Third-Out or 1.5L
  • Unsecured: Converts to equity or deeply subordinated
  • Management: MIP (5-10% equity pool)
Structure 3: Amend-and-Extend with Governance Rights
  • Existing documents amended to extend maturities, modify covenants, adjust rates (add PIK toggle), and add governance provisions
  • Lender benefit: Lower execution risk, faster implementation (2-4 weeks), governance rights for monitoring
  • Company benefit: Least disruptive option, no equity conversion, extended runway, can be characterized as routine refinancing
  • Governance rights extracted: Board observer/seat, monthly financial reporting, consent rights over additional debt/asset sales/management changes, minimum liquidity covenant, springing covenants that tighten over time
Structure 4: Equitization (Debt-for-Equity Conversion)
  • Some or all debt converts to equity, dramatically deleveraging the balance sheet
  • Lender benefit: Eliminates debt overhang; equity upside if business recovers; board control
  • Company benefit: Eliminates cash interest payments; resets capital structure for long-term viability
  • AMC caution: The retail shareholder base creates political and legal challenges. A full equity wipe-out generates significant backlash. A partial equitization where existing shareholders retain 5-15% may be more feasible.
Structure 5: Drop-Down Transaction (What AMC Did)
  • Valuable assets transferred to newly created unrestricted subsidiary; new debt issued at subsidiary secured by transferred assets; non-participating creditors left with claims against asset-depleted parent
  • AMC’s July 2024 transaction: 175 domestic theaters + IP transferred to Muvico; excluded 1L noteholders (7.5% due 2029) sued; settled July 2025 with TSA
  • Legal risks: Litigation from excluded creditors is nearly guaranteed; key issues are “permitted investment” baskets, restricted payment covenants, and intercreditor agreement compliance
Structure 6: Sale-Leaseback / Asset Monetization
  • AMC leases most theaters (limiting sale-leaseback), but owns some properties and has valuable IP, brand licensing, and technology platforms (AMC Stubs, AMC+)
  • A creditor proposal could include monetizing these assets to provide liquidity
4. Negotiation Dynamics
Approaching Management: The “Cooperative Strength” Framework
  1. Lead with the business case, not legal threats. Open with: “We believe in the long-term viability of AMC’s business. We are prepared to provide the capital and runway needed to capitalize on the box office recovery.”
  2. Present a comprehensive solution, not demands. Show a fully-modeled proposal with pro forma balance sheet, liquidity runway, and debt service coverage.
  3. Make the “no deal” scenario clear but not threatening. Include a comparison: “Under the status quo, the company faces $X billion maturity wall with only $Y million liquidity. Our proposal extends maturities and injects $Z million.”
  4. Offer management a personal stake. MIP, employment agreements, severance protections convert management from adversary to aligned partner.
  5. Create time pressure without ultimatums. “Our commitment to provide new money is available for [X weeks]” – preserves dignity while creating urgency.
The Fulcrum Security

The fulcrum security is the tranche where enterprise value “breaks” – the tranche that would be partially (not fully) recovered in liquidation/reorganization. This tranche’s holders will ultimately own the post-restructuring entity.

For AMC: At ~$387M EBITDA and a 9-10x multiple, EV is approximately $3.5-4.0B. Total debt is ~$4.3B. The fulcrum likely sits in the 2L notes or at the bottom of the 1L complex. This has direct implications for ad hoc group composition and transaction structure.

Coalition building: Start with 3-5 aligned holders (typically distressed funds), reach Required Lender threshold (50%+), address CLO concerns by offering par exchange options, execute cooperation agreement with lockup/transfer/new-money provisions, and design holdout economics to be inferior to participating economics.

Managing the Timeline

Speed matters when: Approaching maturities, rapidly deteriorating liquidity, competitive creditor dynamics, favorable market conditions, seasonal business needs (AMC: major movie release slate).

Patience pays when: Waiting for covenant violation increases leverage, allowing management to exhaust alternatives, building larger coalition, waiting for better information, letting litigation threats mature.

Typical benchmarks: Formation to term sheet (3-6 weeks), term sheet to documentation (4-8 weeks), solicitation period (20-30 business days), total process (2-5 months).

Information Rights

Creditors need and should push for: monthly financial statements, weekly 13-week cash flow forecasts, covenant compliance certificates, capital expenditure tracking, theater-level P&L reporting (AMC-specific), detailed operating budgets with variance reporting, and pending litigation exposure summaries.

5. Governance and Control Mechanisms
Board Representation
  • Observer rights: Lower friction; creditor attends all meetings, receives all materials, but does not vote. Frame as “adding experienced perspectives.”
  • Full board seats: Typical in exchange offers or major new money transactions. Creditor-designated directors have fiduciary duties to the company. Tie to debt thresholds (“so long as group holds $X of debt”).
  • Precedent: Del Monte excluded lenders (Black Diamond) actually replaced the entire board through voting pledged stock – an extreme but effective outcome.
Enhanced Monitoring and Consent Rights

Reporting: Monthly unaudited financials, weekly rolling cash forecasts, monthly covenant certificates, immediate notice of defaults, annual budgets with quarterly variance reports.

Consent rights over: Additional indebtedness, asset sales/transfers (prevents future drop-downs without approval), business strategy changes, M&A, restricted payments, management changes, capital expenditures above budget, related-party transactions.

Management change provisions: Creditor consent for CEO/CFO appointment or removal; key-person triggers; springing provisions if financial metrics are missed (more board seats, tighter consent rights).

Anti-Dilution and Equity Protections (if equitization is part of the deal)
  • Anti-dilution protection (weighted-average or full-ratchet)
  • Pre-emptive rights in future issuances
  • Registration rights for public resale
  • Tag-along rights
  • Put rights at specified triggers (change of control, milestone failure)
6. The Dual Track Strategy
Why Dual-Track Is the Gold Standard

Simultaneously pursue Track 1 (out-of-court LME) and Track 2 (in-court restructuring). The credible threat of bankruptcy makes management more willing to agree to out-of-court terms. If negotiations fail, the creditor group is already prepared for court.

7. Recent Examples and Precedents
AMC Entertainment (July 2024 LME)
  • Transaction: Drop-down of 175 domestic theaters + IP to Muvico (unrestricted subsidiary)
  • Excluded: $950M 1L notes (7.5% due 2029)
  • Litigation: NY state court breach claims (Sept 2024)
  • Resolution: July 2025 TSA – excluded noteholders provided new money and exchanged into third-priority Muvico debt
  • Credit metrics: Total debt $4,307M to $4,331M (unchanged); net leverage 9.1x to 9.2x; near-term maturities $2,781M to $362M (primary goal achieved)
  • Lesson: Primarily a maturity management exercise, not a deleveraging. Excluded creditors ultimately made out well through litigation leverage.
Del Monte Foods (August 2024 LME)
  • Transaction: Drop-down of substantially ALL assets to DMFC II; $240M new money
  • Excluded: ~15% holdouts led by Black Diamond
  • Litigation: Delaware Section 225 proceeding (novel – replaced board through voting pledged stock)
  • Settlement: April 2025 – excluded lenders repaid at par plus $20M fees
  • Outcome: Filed Chapter 11 in July 2025 despite the LME
  • Key lessons: (1) Delaware Section 225 approach was faster than NY breach litigation; (2) excluded lenders arguably came out better than participants; (3) LMEs providing only short-term liquidity fixes may just delay bankruptcy; (4) new money is critical
Serta Simmons Bedding (2020 Uptier – Landmark)
  • Transaction: Non-pro-rata uptier creating super-priority first lien, subordinating minority lenders
  • Fifth Circuit ruling: Uptier was NOT a violation of sacred pro rata rights, but WAS a violation of the “open market purchase” exception
  • Impact: Created significant uncertainty; credit agreements now include “Serta blockers” limiting non-pro-rata transactions
J.Crew (2016 – Original Drop-Down)
  • Transferred $250M of IP to unrestricted subsidiary through permitted investment baskets
  • Exploited the “J.Crew trapdoor” (non-guarantor restricted sub basket + proceeds basket)
  • Generated the “J.Crew blocker” now standard in credit agreements
  • AMC and Del Monte later followed this template despite tighter post-J.Crew documentation
Common Factors in Successful Lender-Management Cooperation
  1. Mutual recognition that bankruptcy would be worse for everyone
  2. New money gave management something tangible
  3. MIPs aligned management incentives
  4. Early engagement before liquidity crisis became acute
  5. Credible advisors on both sides facilitated trust
  6. Clear milestones via RSAs kept process on track
  7. Holdout management via structural subordination minimized free-rider problems
What Led to Adversarial Outcomes
  1. Surprise exclusion from transactions without warning
  2. Value destruction benefiting insiders at creditor expense
  3. Management refusal to engage
  4. Sponsor interference prioritizing equity recovery

Appendix: Key Legal Concepts

Sacred Rights: Require unanimous consent to amend – maturity extension, principal reduction, interest rate reduction, pro rata sharing changes, release of all/substantially all collateral or guarantees.

Required Lender: Typically >50% of outstanding loans. Can approve amendments, direct agent action, accelerate debt.

Permitted Investments / Restricted Payments: Credit agreement baskets that LMEs exploit to transfer assets to unrestricted subsidiaries, incur subsidiary-level debt, and make intercompany loans.

Intercreditor Agreements: Govern priority of liens/payments between creditor classes, standstill periods, turnover provisions, and DIP consent rights. AMC’s intercreditor agreement between 1L and 2L was central to the excluded noteholder litigation.

Open Market Purchase Exception: Allows debt repurchase without pro rata offering to all lenders. The Fifth Circuit’s Serta decision narrowed this exception, finding privately negotiated exchanges with select lenders do not qualify.
